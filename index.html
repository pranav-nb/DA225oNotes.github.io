Deep Learning and Generative AI Engineering — Overview

一、Course Overview

This course offers a modern, practitioner-oriented treatment of deep learning through the lens of generative AI, large language models, and agentic systems. Rather than surveying classical architectures in historical sequence, it grounds every concept in a single unifying question: given a real-world business need, how do you decide whether AI is necessary, which parts of the solution require it, and how do you build, evaluate, and maintain that solution at production quality?

The course opens by establishing what artificial intelligence actually is—and, equally important, what it is not. A rigorous definition anchored in the ability of a digital system to acquire and apply knowledge is applied to everyday scenarios, from hospital reception desks to pizza ordering, live translation, and smart air conditioning. Three diagnostic questions emerge as a practical talisman: Does the task require effort or training for a human to perform? Was the system's behaviour specified directly in code, or did it emerge through learning? Can the system make autonomous decisions? These questions recur throughout the course as the lens through which every technical choice is evaluated.

From that foundation, the course develops an input-output mental model of machine learning. Every AI sub-problem is characterised by the dimensionality of its input space and output space. When both are narrow and fixed, rule-based automation suffices. When either expands toward natural language, images, or audio—modalities whose combinatorial richness is effectively infinite—machine learning becomes essential, and specifically the transformer architecture that has consolidated the field over the past seven years.

The technical core then unfolds in two complementary directions. On one track, the course examines how transformers power generative models: the mechanics of autoregressive token generation, the concept of vocabulary and softmax selection, the distinction between autoregressive and diffusion-based generation, and the relationship between language models, vision models, audio models, and multimodal systems. On the other track, it introduces the training pipeline that turns a raw transformer into a usable product: pre-training on vast corpora through tasks like masked language modelling and next-token prediction, followed by post-training through instruction fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback.

The course culminates in agentic AI and specification-driven development. Students learn to architect multi-agent systems—complete with sub-agents, tool access, memory buffers, and guardrails—and to build them not through ad-hoc coding but through a disciplined workflow: write a specification, let an AI coding agent plan and implement, review diffs, and verify against explicit test cases. The six-step AI/ML workflow, from problem framing through deployment and monitoring, provides the scaffolding for a capstone project in which teams develop a working agentic application end to end.

二、Learning Objectives

This course aims to equip learners with a systematic ability to evaluate whether a given task genuinely requires AI or can be solved through simpler automation, and to architect end-to-end solutions accordingly. By the conclusion of the course, learners will have internalised the input-output mental model that connects business requirements to technical choices, and will be able to decompose a complex business need into sub-problems, identify which sub-problems demand machine learning, and specify the data modality and output cardinality for each.

On the technical side, learners will develop a first-principles understanding of the transformer architecture—from tokenisation through attention mechanisms to decoding—sufficient to explain the entire pipeline from memory even under pressure. They will understand how foundation models are created through pre-training and refined through post-training, and will be able to reason about when prompt engineering, retrieval-augmented generation, fine-tuning, or development from scratch is the appropriate strategy for a given use case.

Equally important, learners will develop the "taste" that separates effective AI engineers from mere prompt operators: the ability to judge whether a model's output is correct, to write specifications that produce reliable software, to define meaningful evaluation metrics, and to communicate results to stakeholders through data-driven narratives. The course treats specification-driven development not as a shortcut but as the emerging professional standard for AI-assisted software engineering.

三、Course Structure

The course is organised into fifteen chapters, each building on the preceding material while remaining self-contained enough for targeted review.

The first chapter establishes what artificial intelligence is through a precise definition and three diagnostic questions, working through concrete scenarios to build intuition about when AI is genuinely necessary. The second chapter introduces the input-output mental model and the taxonomy of data modalities—tabular, time series, image, video, text, audio, and scientific data—that defines the space of possible AI tasks. The third chapter explores the distinction between predictive and generative AI, explaining how the dimensionality of the output space determines the fundamental nature of the problem and introducing autoregressive and diffusion-based generation.

The fourth chapter focuses on large language models and the foundation model paradigm, covering tokens, vocabulary, transformers, pre-training, post-training, and the distinction between prompt engineering, retrieval-augmented generation, and fine-tuning. The fifth chapter brings these ideas together in the context of agentic AI and conversational commerce, examining how autonomous systems use sub-agents, tools, and memory to pursue goals in domains like automated barista applications and customer support. The sixth chapter presents the specification-driven development methodology and the six-step AI/ML workflow that structures the entire journey from business need through deployment and monitoring.

The seventh chapter makes specification-driven development concrete through the mechanics of Claude Code: the three-phase agentic loop, the CLAUDE.md memory file, spec.md anatomy, context management, skills, and the critical concept of AI slop—output that looks professional but is substantively wrong. The eighth chapter addresses tokenization, the essential first step of the transformer pipeline, covering byte pair encoding for text and patchification for images, and demonstrating how classical computer vision and NLP tasks have collapsed into a unified question-answering framework powered by a single architecture.

The ninth chapter develops the training procedure from first principles: neurons as rotation-and-translation units, parameter counting for dense layers, loss functions (cross-entropy and mean squared error), and gradient descent explained through a memorable mountain-descent analogy. It establishes the development-testing paradigm that protects against overfitting and draws the fundamental distinction between optimisation (minimising training loss) and learning (finding parameter states that generalise). The tenth chapter presents the transformer's self-attention mechanism step by step, from context-agnostic embeddings through the query-key-value projection, scaled dot-product scoring, softmax normalisation, and weighted value summation that produces context-aware representations, completing the technical core of the course.

The eleventh chapter assembles every component into the complete GPT pipeline: embedding with positional encoding, stacked transformer decoder blocks with skip connections and layer normalization for training stability, the language model head that produces vocabulary-dimensional logits, and the temperature-controlled top-K/top-P sampling strategies that generate tokens during inference. The twelfth chapter covers the post-training pipeline—supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback with reward models and verifiable rewards—and then introduces context engineering through retrieval-augmented generation, comparing sparse (term-based) and dense (embedding-based) retrieval methods and tracing the evolution from naive RAG through hybrid RAG to the agentic RAG pattern that represents the current state of the art.

The thirteenth chapter systematises agentic AI into a pattern library across three levels of agency: augmented LLMs (Level 1), workflow patterns including chaining, routing, parallelisation, orchestrator-sub-agent, and evaluator-optimiser (Level 2), and autonomous agents with single and multi-agent configurations (Level 3). It establishes the cardinal rule—start at the lowest level that solves the problem—and provides the decision framework for choosing the right level of autonomy. The fourteenth chapter addresses interoperability standards: the Model Context Protocol (MCP) for agent-tool communication via servers and clients, the Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol for cross-framework agent delegation, and the practical implementation of agent loops using the Anthropic SDK, including a worked debugging example that illuminates the challenges at the boundary between natural language reasoning and deterministic tool execution.

The fifteenth chapter addresses the evaluation of LLM applications—a discipline fundamentally different from traditional software testing. It introduces the Three Gulfs mental model (comprehension, specification, generalisation) that explains why LLM pipelines fail, and develops the Analyse-Measure-Improve loop: building failure taxonomies through open and axial coding on sampled traces, designing evaluators (code checks, code-plus-lookup, LLM-as-judge) for each named failure mode, and improving through prioritised fixes with regression verification. It establishes that the taxonomy of failure is the deliverable from which all metrics and improvements flow.

Chapter 1: What Is Artificial Intelligence

一、Introduction and Overview

Before any discussion of deep learning architectures, training procedures, or deployment pipelines, there is a more fundamental question that every practitioner must be able to answer with precision: what, exactly, is artificial intelligence? The question matters not because it is academic, but because the answer determines where engineering effort should be invested. In a landscape saturated with AI branding—AI pens, AI air conditioners, AI-labelled features that amount to simple database lookups—the ability to distinguish genuine AI from marketing hype is itself a professional skill.

This chapter establishes a working definition of artificial intelligence, introduces three diagnostic questions for determining whether a given task requires an AI solution, and works through a series of concrete examples that illustrate how the same business need can be addressed with or without AI depending on design choices. The goal is not to memorise a taxonomy but to develop an intuition that can be applied to any new scenario.

二、Core Concepts and Principles

1. Defining Artificial Intelligence

Artificial intelligence is the ability of a digital computer or a computer-controlled robot to perform tasks that are commonly associated with intelligent beings. This definition, while broad, becomes precise when paired with an understanding of what "intelligent beings" actually do. Natural intelligence is defined as the ability to acquire and apply knowledge. A child learns what a cat looks like by being shown examples—some fur, four legs, whiskers, a tail—and can then apply that knowledge in a novel situation, correctly identifying a new cat or correctly rejecting a dog. If a digital system can replicate this cycle of acquisition and application, that system exhibits artificial intelligence.

The critical distinction lies in how the system arrives at its behaviour. A rule-based system where a programmer directly encodes the decision logic—if temperature exceeds 37°C and SpO₂ falls below 95%, administer oxygen—is automation, but it is not artificial intelligence in the modern sense. The rules were specified by a human; they did not emerge from data. Conversely, if the system learns those thresholds by examining thousands of past triage decisions, discovering the boundary between "send home" and "admit to ICU" from the patterns in the data, then the system has acquired knowledge and can apply it. That is AI.

This distinction matters practically because automation and AI have very different cost profiles, failure modes, and maintenance requirements. A rule-based thermostat is cheap, predictable, and requires no training data. An AI system that adjusts room temperature by perceiving occupancy through cameras, measuring body heat, reasoning about comfort, and making autonomous adjustments is far more capable but also far more expensive and complex. The choice between them is a design decision, not a moral one.

2. Narrow Intelligence and General Intelligence

Artificial intelligence can be narrow or general. Narrow AI performs a specific task that an intelligent human could perform—classifying X-ray images as fractured or not, translating speech from one language to another, detecting fraud in credit card transactions. Each of these tasks requires years of training for a human to master, and an AI system that can perform the task at a comparable level represents genuine artificial intelligence, but only within that narrow domain.

General intelligence, by contrast, would be a system capable of performing the full spectrum of tasks that humans can perform—the kind of system depicted in science fiction, where a robot can cook, clean, hold conversations, perform surgery, and compose music. Whether current frontier models like Opus have achieved some form of general intelligence is a matter of ongoing debate that depends heavily on how "general intelligence" is defined. What is clear is that the practical AI systems being built and deployed today are narrow systems, and the engineering discipline required to build them well is the subject of this course.

3. Three Diagnostic Questions

Three questions, applied systematically, can determine whether a given task requires an AI solution.

The first question asks whether the task requires effort or training for a human to perform. An orthopedic surgeon who can look at a patient's gait and clinical symptoms and determine—without even examining the X-ray—that no cast is needed has accumulated perhaps fifteen years of training: school preparation, medical entrance exams, undergraduate medical education, internship, postgraduate specialisation, and fellowship. When a task demands that level of human expertise, automating it to a comparable standard is a genuine AI challenge. By contrast, checking someone into a hospital by collecting their name, age, and Aadhaar number from a keyboard requires no special training and no AI.

The second question asks whether the system's behaviour was directly specified in code or emerged indirectly through learning or search. The COVID-era triage system that applied fixed threshold rules—temperature above 37°C and SpO₂ below 95 triggers oxygen supplementation—had its behaviour directly specified by a human programmer. Even though it looks like an intelligent system from the outside (perceiving the patient, making a decision), it is fundamentally different from a system that discovered those thresholds by analysing thousands of patient outcomes. The indirect emergence of behaviour, whether through training on labelled data or searching through a knowledge base, is a hallmark of genuine AI.

The third question asks whether the system can make autonomous decisions with some degree of flexibility and adaptability. A fully self-driving car that processes visual input, reasons about how the traffic scene will evolve, and autonomously controls steering, acceleration, and braking to navigate from point A to point B without an accident represents the highest level of AI: perception, world modelling, decision-making, and action, all in a closed loop. Today's agentic AI coding systems, like Claude Code, represent a parallel form of autonomous decision-making in the software development domain—perceiving the state of a codebase, reasoning about what changes are needed, implementing them, testing, and iterating.

三、Practical Case Studies

Case Study 1: Hospital Outpatient Department Reception

At a hospital reception desk, patients must register, book appointments, and be directed to the appropriate department. The backend is a structured database requiring name, age, identification details, and department selection. If the computer-human interface is a keyboard and dropdown menus, no AI is needed—the entire system is a standard web or mobile application with predetermined options.

AI enters the picture at the interface layer. If patients show physical ID cards to a camera, the system must process Aadhaar cards, driving licenses (which look different across thirty states), passports from 186 countries, PAN cards, and ration cards. The input space is enormously wide, and a human would need training to handle all these formats. That image-to-text conversion step is an AI task. Similarly, if a patient says "I broke my leg" instead of selecting "Orthopaedics" from a dropdown, natural language understanding is needed to map the infinite space of possible utterances to the finite set of departments. The output interface can also benefit from AI: converting displayed text into speech for accessibility requires text-to-speech conversion, another AI task. The key insight is that AI necessity is determined by the interface design—specifically, by whether the input or output modality has a wide or narrow space.

Case Study 2: Live Translation of a Political Rally

A speaker addresses a crowd in Hindi, and the speech must be translated in real time into Tamil for another audience. The input space is wide (any possible utterance in the source language), and the output space is equally wide (any possible utterance in the target language). Rule-based word-by-word translation—forming a sentence in the source language and mechanically substituting each word—produces grammatically awkward output that may convey meaning but lacks the naturalness a listener expects. Many people experience this daily when speaking a second language: sentences formed in the mother tongue and translated word-by-word sound "weird" to native speakers.

Because both the input and output spaces are effectively infinite, and because a human translator requires years of training to perform the task fluently, this is unambiguously an AI task. The system must acquire knowledge of how both languages work—not just vocabulary mappings but grammar, idiom, context, and pragmatics—and apply that knowledge in real time.

Case Study 3: Ordering Pizza at Domino's

The output space for a pizza order is constrained: perhaps ten pizza varieties, five sizes, and a handful of extras. If the interface is a set of radio buttons—select size, select toppings, confirm—no AI is needed. The input space is fixed, the output space is fixed, and a simple web form handles the entire interaction.

AI becomes relevant only if the interface shifts to natural language. A customer typing "give me a large pepper paneer pizza" requires natural language understanding to map from the wide input space to the narrow output space. Whether this added complexity is worth the cost depends on whether the natural language interface genuinely improves the customer experience compared to radio buttons—and for most pizza-ordering scenarios, the answer is probably no.

Case Study 4: Temperature Control in an AC Room

A standard air conditioner uses a thermostat: a temperature sensor checks whether the room is above the set point, and the compressor runs until it is not. This is classical control theory—no AI involved, despite what marketing materials may claim.

A genuinely intelligent air conditioner would need to perceive the room through cameras and sensors—detecting occupancy levels, measuring body temperature, assessing humidity—and make autonomous decisions about how to adjust temperature over time for optimal comfort. It might blast cold air for ten minutes when a room full of people is initially warm, then ease back as the room cools. That perception-reasoning-action loop, with flexible decision-making and environmental adaptation, would constitute real AI. But the engineering cost of cameras, depth sensors, LiDAR, and the software to interpret all that data far exceeds the benefit for most residential applications. Knowing this is part of developing taste.

四、Summary

Artificial intelligence is the ability of a digital system to acquire and apply knowledge—to do what previously required human intelligence. Three questions determine whether a specific task needs AI: Does it require human training to perform? Did its behaviour emerge through learning rather than explicit programming? Can the system make autonomous decisions? The answers depend critically on the dimensionality of the input and output spaces. When both are narrow and fixed, rule-based automation suffices. When either expands toward natural language, images, or other high-dimensional modalities, machine learning—and specifically, as the course will show, the transformer architecture—becomes essential. Developing the ability to make this determination quickly and accurately, resisting the industry's tendency to label everything as AI, is the first and perhaps most important skill an AI practitioner can cultivate.

Chapter 2: The Input-Output Mental Model and Data Modalities

一、Introduction and Overview

Every AI solution, regardless of its complexity, ultimately reduces to a relationship between inputs and outputs. A customer query enters a system and a resolution emerges. An X-ray image is fed to a model and a diagnosis comes out. A speech signal arrives and a translated utterance is produced. This chapter formalises that intuition into a mental model that serves as the foundation for all subsequent technical discussion: the input-output framework. It then catalogues the major data modalities—the types of "potatoes" that go in and the types of "gold" that come out—establishing the vocabulary needed to specify any AI sub-problem with precision.

二、The Input-Output Framework

1. Thinking in Terms of Inputs and Outputs

The most powerful abstraction in AI engineering is deceptively simple: for any task you want to automate, identify what goes in and what comes out. This is not merely a technical observation; it is a way of seeing the world. When architecting a solution for a hospital outpatient department, every block in the workflow—registration, department selection, wait-time estimation, screen display—has an input and an output. When designing a customer support system, the chain runs from the customer query through intent detection, agent routing, multi-step resolution, and satisfaction verification, with each link characterised by what it receives and what it produces.

The input-output framing immediately reveals where AI is needed. If both the input space and the output space are narrow and fixed—a dropdown selection mapped to a database entry—the link requires no AI. If either space is wide—natural language, images, audio—then the link requires a model that can learn the mapping from data rather than having it specified by a human programmer. This principle was established through examples in the previous chapter, and it remains the primary tool for solution architecture throughout the course.

2. From Business Need to Sub-Problems

Framing an AI problem begins with a business need, not with a model. A credit card company wants to reduce the time between a customer raising a complaint and its resolution. A Kirana store owner wants to reach more customers without hiring staff. A hospital wants to digitise its patient intake.

From the business need, sub-problems are identified. Some are pure software engineering—building a web form, connecting to a database, setting up a payment gateway. Some are AI problems—understanding natural language queries, classifying customer intent, generating personalised responses. For each AI sub-problem, the practitioner must identify the input modality, the output modality, and the dimensionality of each space. A text classification task has wide input (any customer utterance) and narrow output (one of a fixed set of intent categories). A text generation task has wide input and wide output.

This decomposition is hierarchical. Inside a sub-problem, there may be further sub-problems. Data gathering itself can be an AI task—if it involves labelling images or processing unstructured documents. The architect must be able to zoom in and out, identifying AI needs at every level and specifying each one with enough precision that it can be handed off for development.

三、Data Modalities

1. Tabular Data

Tabular data is the most familiar format in data science. Each row represents a data point—a person, a transaction, a sensor reading—and each column represents a feature. Features can be continuous numbers (age, loan amount, temperature), categorical values (gender, city, marital status), or even complex objects like text documents or images embedded within a cell. A loan default prediction task, for example, might have one row per applicant with columns for income, employment status, number of credit cards, location, and a recommendation letter (text), with the output being a binary classification: will this person default or not?

The smallest unit of tabular data is the cell. The key characteristic of tabular data in the input-output framework is that both the number of features and the number of possible output values are typically fixed at training time, making most tabular tasks predictive rather than generative.

2. Time Series Data

Time series data is tabular data with a temporal ordering. Each entry belongs to a specific point in time, and the sequence matters. Stock prices, sensor readings, weather measurements, and heartbeat signals are all time series. The smallest unit is a single timestep's measurement, but the meaningful unit is typically a window of consecutive measurements.

The temporal structure introduces a key property: patterns in time series data are sequential, and models must capture dependencies across time. This makes time series a natural fit for sequence-based architectures, including transformers.

3. Image Data

An image is a two-dimensional array of pixels, where each pixel records intensity values across one or more channels. A standard photograph captured by a CMOS sensor has three channels—red, green, and blue—corresponding to the three types of colour receptors in the human eye. The width, height, and number of channels together define the shape of one image: a 1080×720 RGB photograph, for example, contains 1080 × 720 × 3 = approximately 2.3 million values.

A pixel is the smallest unit of an image, and each pixel value is typically a number between 0 and 255. What makes image data remarkable from the input-output perspective is its combinatorial richness. A single one-megapixel greyscale image—a million pixels, each with 256 possible values—can represent virtually any visual scene in human experience: a black-and-white photograph of a historical event, a medical X-ray, a satellite view of a city. The space of possible images is so vast that it is effectively infinite, which is precisely why processing images requires AI rather than rule-based systems.

Image data need not be limited to RGB. Multispectral satellite imagery, LiDAR depth maps, radar returns, and medical imaging modalities like CT and MRI all produce image-like data with different numbers of channels, each capturing a different physical quantity. The principle remains the same: spatial information arranged in a grid, with the number of channels determined by the sensing modality.

4. Video Data

Video data is a time series of images—a sequence of frames captured at some number of frames per second. If a single image already has an effectively infinite space of possibilities, a sequence of images multiplies that space further. The smallest unit of video data is a single pixel in a single frame, but the meaningful unit is a temporal sequence of spatial frames.

Video understanding requires models that can reason about both spatial content (what is in each frame) and temporal dynamics (how the scene evolves). Self-driving systems, for example, must process video streams in real time, understanding not just what objects are present but predicting how they will move.

5. Text Data

Text, as humans experience it, is a sequence of characters—letters, spaces, punctuation marks, arranged into words, sentences, and documents. As a computer stores it, text is a sequence of bytes in an encoding format like UTF-8. Each character maps to one or more bytes, and the model must ultimately work with these numerical representations.

The combinatorial richness of text is staggering. A person with a vocabulary of 2,000 words can construct a 100-word sentence in 2,000¹⁰⁰ possible ways—a number so large it is effectively infinite. Even a 10-word sentence permits 2,000¹⁰ combinations. This is why any task involving natural language as input or output is inherently an AI task: the space of possibilities is far too vast for rules to be manually specified.

Text is also the modality through which humans store and transmit most of their knowledge. Combined with images, text and visual data together represent the primary media of human learning and communication. This observation has profound implications for AI: a model that can process both text and images has access to the two modalities that encode the bulk of human knowledge.

6. Audio Data

Audio is a pressure wave—vibrations in air caused by a sound source (a voice, a musical instrument, an engine) that travel to a receiver (an ear, a microphone). When a microphone captures audio, it converts the continuous analogue pressure wave into a discrete digital signal: a sequence of amplitude values sampled at regular intervals. Speech, music, environmental sounds, and industrial noise are all audio data.

The smallest unit of audio data is a single sample—one amplitude measurement at one point in time. Like text, audio is sequential: the order of samples matters, and meaning emerges from patterns across time. Processing audio for tasks like speech recognition, speaker identification, or music generation requires models that can reason about these temporal patterns.

7. Scientific and Graph Data

Scientific data encompasses modalities that do not fit neatly into the categories above. Weather data, for example, might include temperature, humidity, pressure, and wind speed measured at every point on a three-dimensional grid (latitude, longitude, altitude) across multiple time steps. Molecular data represents atoms as nodes and chemical bonds as edges in a graph structure. Materials science data might combine spectroscopic measurements with crystal structure information.

Graph data is less a separate modality than a way of representing relationships. Any data type can be structured as a graph: pixels in an image are connected to their neighbours, words in a sentence are connected by syntactic relationships, and intersections in a traffic network are connected by roads. When the relational structure is itself important for the task—predicting molecular properties from atomic connectivity, for example—graph-based representations become essential.

四、Cardinality, Vocabulary, and the Space of Possibilities

The concept of cardinality—the number of distinct options available for a data element—ties the data modalities back to the input-output mental model. A categorical feature with two values (yes/no) has cardinality 2. A feature with four categories (male child, female child, male adult, female adult) has cardinality 4. A continuous variable like rainfall in millimetres has, in principle, infinite cardinality, though in practice it can be discretised into bins.

Language operates through vocabulary: a fixed set of words from which utterances are composed. A vocabulary of 2,000 words is a categorical variable with cardinality 2,000. But because sentences are sequences of variable length, the effective cardinality of the output space grows exponentially: 2,000¹⁰ for a 10-word sentence, 2,000¹⁰⁰ for a 100-word sentence. This is the precise sense in which text has an "infinite" output space. Images work similarly: a 1,000×1,000 greyscale image has 256 raised to the power of one million possible configurations.

This exponential explosion is what makes generative tasks fundamentally different from predictive tasks. When the output is one of a small set of categories, the problem is classification. When the output is a point in an effectively infinite space, the problem is generation. The next chapter examines this distinction in detail.

五、Summary

The input-output mental model transforms AI problem-solving from an intuitive art into a structured engineering discipline. By identifying the modality and dimensionality of both inputs and outputs for every sub-problem in a solution architecture, practitioners can determine precisely where AI is needed, what type of model is appropriate, and where simpler automation will suffice. The eight data modalities—tabular, time series, image, video, text, audio, scientific, and graph—constitute the vocabulary of this framework, and the concept of cardinality provides the bridge between data types and the fundamental distinction between predictive and generative AI that the course explores next.

Chapter 3: Predictive AI and Generative AI

一、Introduction and Overview

With the input-output mental model and data modalities established, a natural question arises: what fundamentally distinguishes the classical machine learning tasks of regression and classification from the text generation, image synthesis, and audio production that define the current AI landscape? The answer lies in the dimensionality of the output space. This chapter formalises the distinction between predictive AI and generative AI, introduces the two principal mechanisms of generation—autoregressive modelling and diffusion—and establishes the transformer architecture as the common engine that powers both paradigms.

二、Core Concepts and Principles

1. Predictive AI: Fixed-Dimensional Outputs

Predictive AI encompasses the tasks that dominated machine learning for most of its history. The input can be any data modality—tabular, time series, image, video, text, audio, graph, or scientific data. The defining characteristic is the output: a continuous or categorical variable of fixed dimension, determined at training time.

A credit card default prediction system, for example, takes one row of tabular data—income, employment, credit history, number of cards, location, perhaps even a text-based recommendation letter—and produces a single binary output: default or not. A regression model might instead predict the maximum loan amount, a single continuous number. Even when predicting multiple values simultaneously—say, ten different risk scores—the output vector has a dimension that is fixed before training begins. This is what "predictive" means: the model predicts a specific, bounded quantity.

Classification with C classes, regression of one number, regression of K numbers, or prediction on a fixed spatial grid are all predictive tasks. The output space is finite and known in advance. Most of classical machine learning—support vector machines, random forests, XGBoost, logistic regression, and early neural networks—falls squarely in this category.

2. Generative AI: Infinite-Dimensional Outputs

Generative AI differs from predictive AI in a single fundamental respect: the output space is effectively infinite. When a language model generates text, the output can be any sequence of any length, composed from any combination of vocabulary tokens. When an image model generates a picture, the output can be any arrangement of pixels at any resolution. The same principle applies to audio generation, video generation, and code generation.

The term "unstructured data" historically referred to precisely these modalities—text, images, video, audio—because their dimensionality is not fixed in advance. The same photograph can exist as a 1-megapixel, 10-megapixel, or 60-megapixel image. A text can be one word or one million words. A video can last three seconds or three hours. This variability in dimensionality is what makes the output space "infinite" in practical terms.

To appreciate the scale of this space, consider language generation with a vocabulary of 20,000 words. A three-word sentence has 20,000³ = 8 × 10¹² possible combinations. A five-word sentence has 20,000⁵ ≈ 3.2 × 10²¹ possibilities. Scaling to a vocabulary of 200,000 tokens and sentence lengths of hundreds or thousands of tokens produces numbers that dwarf the number of atoms in the observable universe. The output space of a generative model is, for all practical purposes, infinite.

3. The Autoregressive Generation Mechanism

How does a model produce output in an infinite-dimensional space using a finite-state digital computer? The answer, for autoregressive models, is: one token at a time.

The process begins with an input—a prompt consisting of a sequence of tokens. This sequence is fed into a neural network called a transformer. The transformer processes all input tokens and produces a probability distribution over the vocabulary—a softmax over, say, 20,000 possible next tokens. One token is selected from this distribution and appended to the input sequence. The extended sequence is then fed back into the transformer, which produces a new probability distribution for the next position. This cycle repeats: generate one token, append it, feed the extended sequence back in, generate the next token.

This loop continues until one of two conditions is met. Either the sequence reaches a pre-specified maximum length, or the model generates a special end-of-sequence (EOS) token that signals completion. The EOS token is reserved specifically for this purpose—when the model "decides" that the response is complete, it generates EOS, and the loop terminates.

An untrained model could generate any of the 20,000 tokens at each position with roughly equal probability, producing gibberish. The entire purpose of training—both pre-training and post-training—is to "tame" this machine so that each generated token is a sensible continuation of everything that came before. The generated sequence must be coherent, relevant, and useful to humans. Controlling this generation process is the central challenge of deep learning for language models.

4. From Language Models to Multimodal Models

If the vocabulary from which the model generates tokens consists of language tokens—words or subword units—the system is a language model. If the vocabulary consists of image tokens—pixels or patches—it is an image model. If it generates from an audio vocabulary, it is an audio model. If the vocabulary combines tokens from multiple modalities—text tokens alongside image tokens, for example—the system is a multimodal model.

The architecture remains the same in all cases: a transformer that processes a sequence of input tokens and generates output tokens autoregressively. What changes is the vocabulary and the tokenisation scheme—how raw data (characters, pixels, waveform samples) is converted into the discrete tokens that the model operates on. This unification under a single architecture is one of the most significant developments in modern AI.

The term "large language model" (LLM) adds one additional dimension: size. If the transformer has a very large number of parameters—the internal weights that are adjusted during training—it is "large." What counts as large depends on the era: today, models with fewer than 3 billion parameters might be considered small, while 5-10 billion parameters and above qualifies as large. The "L" in LLM refers strictly to the size of the neural network, not to the size of the training data or the length of generated text.

GPT—Generative Pre-trained Transformer—encodes all three concepts in its name. "Generative" because it produces output in the infinite-dimensional space of token sequences. "Pre-trained" because the transformer is first trained on vast amounts of data before being adapted for specific tasks. "Transformer" because that is the neural network architecture that makes everything work.

5. Diffusion: Generation Without Sequence

Autoregressive generation produces tokens one at a time in a fixed order. Diffusion models take an entirely different approach: they generate all tokens simultaneously through a process of iterative denoising.

In image generation, for example, a diffusion model starts with pure noise—random pixel values—and progressively refines this noise into a coherent image through many small denoising steps. There is no sequential ordering over the spatial dimension; the entire image materialises gradually, like a photograph developing in a chemical bath.

Diffusion models can also be applied to language. A diffusion language model generates all tokens in a document simultaneously, refining them through denoising steps rather than producing them one by one.

For much of the recent history of image generation, diffusion was the dominant paradigm. The shift came when companies began releasing autoregressive image generation models—systems that treat image generation as a token-by-token sequential process, similar to text generation. The viral "Ghibli moment," when users flooded social media with images of themselves rendered in Japanese animation style, marked a cultural inflection point for autoregressive image generation.

Today, both autoregressive and diffusion approaches are used for both text and image generation, often in combination. The core engine in both cases is the transformer architecture. The choice between autoregressive and diffusion generation depends on the specific requirements of the task—latency, quality, controllability, and the modality being generated.

三、Connecting the Paradigms

1. Predictive Tasks as Special Cases of Generation

The boundary between predictive and generative AI is porous. Image classification—given an image, output one label from a fixed set—is, in the input-output framework, an image-to-text task where the text is always one word long and comes from a predetermined set. It is a predictive task dressed in generative clothing.

Modern vision-language models can perform image classification as a text generation task: show the model an image, ask "what is this?", and generate the answer as text. This works, but it is, as the analogy goes, bringing a bazooka to a small fight. A simpler classification model is cheaper, faster, and often more accurate for a narrowly defined classification task.

The practical insight is that every computer vision task can today be framed as a vision-language generation problem—describe the scene, identify objects, detect anomalies, all through text generation. Classically, these were treated as regression or classification problems with specialised architectures. The trend is toward unification under generative models, but practitioners should match the tool to the task rather than defaulting to the most powerful available model.

2. Code Generation as a Constrained Text Task

Code generation is a text-to-text task where the output happens to be in a programming language rather than natural language. Because programming languages have stricter syntax and narrower valid output spaces than natural language, the generation problem is somewhat more tractable. Language models post-trained on code—like the Claude family of models—can be particularly effective because the output space, while still vast, is more "tameable" than unrestricted natural language.

This makes code generation one of the most successful practical applications of generative AI, and it forms the basis for the specification-driven development methodology that the course adopts as its central pedagogical and professional framework.

四、Summary

The distinction between predictive and generative AI rests on the dimensionality of the output space. Predictive AI produces fixed-dimensional outputs—classifications, regressions, scores—using the full range of classical and modern machine learning. Generative AI produces outputs in effectively infinite-dimensional spaces—text, images, audio, video—using either autoregressive token-by-token generation or diffusion-based simultaneous denoising. Both paradigms are powered by the transformer architecture, and the choice between them (or their combination) depends on the specific requirements of the task. Understanding this distinction enables practitioners to select the appropriate approach for each sub-problem in a solution architecture, avoiding both the overhead of generative models where prediction suffices and the limitations of predictive models where generation is required.

Chapter 4: Foundation Models and the Training Pipeline

一、Introduction and Overview

The previous chapter established that modern generative AI works by taming a transformer model so that its outputs are coherent, relevant, and useful. This chapter examines how that taming occurs. The journey from a randomly initialised neural network to a model capable of writing code, answering questions, translating languages, and following complex instructions involves two distinct phases: pre-training and post-training. Together, they produce what is known as a foundation model—a large-scale AI model with broad capabilities that can be adapted to a wide range of downstream tasks. Understanding this pipeline is essential for knowing when prompt engineering will suffice, when retrieval-augmented generation is needed, and when fine-tuning or development from scratch becomes necessary.

二、Core Concepts and Principles

1. What Is a Foundation Model?

A foundation model is a large-scale AI model that has been pre-trained on vast amounts of diverse data. The term "foundation" reflects the model's role: it provides a base of broad capabilities—language understanding, pattern recognition, general knowledge—upon which specific applications can be built. Foundation models are not designed for any single task; instead, they develop general competencies through exposure to internet-scale data during pre-training, and are then refined for specific behaviours during post-training.

Examples of foundation models include the Claude family from Anthropic, the GPT family from OpenAI, the Gemini family from Google, and the Grok family from xAI. Each has been pre-trained on massive datasets and post-trained to excel at different capabilities. Claude models, for instance, have undergone extensive post-training for high-quality code generation. Gemini models, building on Google's search engine heritage, have been post-trained to excel at question answering and general-purpose information retrieval.

2. Pre-Training: Learning from Scale

Pre-training is the first phase of creating a foundation model. The core idea is remarkably simple: train the transformer to predict missing or next elements in sequences of data.

For language models, the two primary pre-training tasks are masked language modelling and next-token prediction. In masked language modelling, words are randomly removed from sentences and the model learns to predict what belongs in each blank. "IASc is located in ____" should be completed with "Bangalore." "I eat ____ from a bowl" should be completed with "food." In next-token prediction (completion), the model sees the beginning of a sentence and predicts the next word. Given "Large scale AI model," the next word should be "trained." Given "Large scale AI model trained," the next word should be "on."

These tasks seem trivially simple, yet they force the model to develop a deep representation of language. To predict that "Bangalore" fills the blank in "IASc is located in ____," the model must have learned facts about IASc's location. To predict that "trained" follows "Large scale AI model," it must have learned something about how AI systems are discussed. Scaled to trillions of tokens of diverse text—books, articles, code, conversations, scientific papers—this simple prediction task produces models with remarkably broad knowledge and linguistic capability.

The same principle applies to other modalities. Image models can be pre-trained to fill in missing patches of images or to complete partially revealed images. Audio models can be pre-trained to reconstruct corrupted audio signals. In each case, the pre-training task is generic—predict what is missing—and the knowledge that emerges is broad.

Pre-training is fundamentally about scale: scale of data, scale of computation, and scale of the model itself. The amount of data processed, the number of GPU hours consumed, and the number of parameters in the transformer all contribute to the breadth and depth of the resulting foundation model. This is why pre-training is conducted by a small number of organisations with the resources to operate at this scale—companies like Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and xAI.

3. The Base Model and Its Limitations

The output of pre-training is a base model (sometimes called a pre-trained model). This model has broad capabilities: it can complete sentences, it has absorbed vast amounts of factual knowledge, and it has learned the statistical patterns of language. However, it has significant limitations.

A base model is fundamentally a completion engine. It excels at continuing text in a way that is statistically consistent with its training data. But it does not inherently know how to follow instructions, answer questions helpfully, refuse harmful requests, or format its outputs in ways that users expect. Early versions of ChatGPT demonstrated these limitations vividly: the model could produce fluent text but frequently gave incorrect answers, misinterpreted instructions, or generated responses that were unhelpful or inappropriate.

When these failures surfaced, the initial response was prompt engineering—the art of asking the model questions in precisely the right way to elicit useful responses. Much like writing code, where the computer does exactly what you tell it to do (and nothing more), prompt engineering treats the foundation model as a system that must be addressed in a particular manner to produce desired outputs. This worked to some extent for tasks like summarisation, style transfer, and simple question answering, but it was fragile and unreliable.

4. Post-Training: Aligning Models with Human Preferences

Post-training is the process of refining a base model so that it behaves in ways that humans find useful, safe, and reliable. It is what transforms a completion engine into an assistant.

The key insight behind post-training came from the feedback that users provided on early model outputs. When people shared screenshots of model failures—incorrect translations, nonsensical answers, inability to count letters in words—they were inadvertently providing training signal. The model developers at major labs recognised the value of this feedback and formalised the process.

Post-training involves collecting data on how the model should respond in various situations and using that data to fine-tune the model. The most important form of post-training is instruction fine-tuning: creating pairs of instructions and desired responses, then training the model to produce the correct response for each instruction. "When someone asks you to generate code, do this. When someone asks what time it is, do that." These instruction-response pairs, expressed in natural language, teach the model to follow directions rather than merely complete text.

Beyond instruction following, post-training incorporates reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) or similar techniques. Humans evaluate model outputs—rating which response is better, flagging harmful content, indicating when the model has been helpful—and this feedback is used to adjust the model's behaviour. The goal is to ensure that the model generates responses that are not just linguistically fluent but genuinely useful, accurate, and aligned with human values.

The companies that build foundation models hire dedicated teams for this work. These are not random internet users but trained evaluators whose job is to provide high-quality feedback. Their evaluations, combined with data from public users, form the training signal for post-training. Positions for this work are available on various gig platforms and through direct hiring, with evaluators selected based on domain expertise.

5. Reasoning Models and Specialisation

Post-training is not a one-size-fits-all process. Different post-training strategies produce models with different strengths. If the post-training data emphasises reasoning—showing the model how to think step by step through complex problems—the resulting model becomes a "reasoning model." DeepSeek's reasoning models, for example, were initially created by distilling outputs from Claude and OpenAI models: prompting the larger models with reasoning tasks, collecting their step-by-step outputs, and using those outputs as training data. This approach—using one model's outputs to train another—is a form of knowledge distillation that has become common in the field.

The distinction between pre-training and post-training is significant because it determines what a model can and cannot do. Pre-training provides broad knowledge and linguistic competency. Post-training shapes how that knowledge is accessed and presented. A model that has not been post-trained for code generation may struggle with programming tasks even if its pre-training data included code. A model that has been extensively post-trained for code—like Claude—can generate, debug, and refactor code with remarkable proficiency.

6. Adapting Foundation Models: Three Strategies

Once a foundation model exists, practitioners have three strategies for making it perform specific tasks.

The first strategy is prompt engineering and context engineering. Without modifying the model's parameters at all, the practitioner crafts input prompts that guide the model toward the desired output. This includes providing examples, specifying output formats, and supplying relevant context. Context engineering—the systematic management of what information is included in the model's input window—is becoming the most valuable practical skill in the field, as it determines how effectively the model can be directed without any retraining.

The second strategy is retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). When the model needs to answer questions based on specific facts or figures that may not be in its training data, relevant information is retrieved from a database or document store and included in the prompt. The model then generates its response using both its pre-trained knowledge and the retrieved context. RAG addresses the problem of factual accuracy without requiring model retraining, making it particularly valuable for applications where information changes frequently.

The third strategy is fine-tuning. When neither prompt engineering nor RAG can achieve the required performance, the model's parameters are adjusted using task-specific data. Fine-tuning is more expensive and complex than prompting or RAG, but it can produce models that are highly specialised for particular domains or tasks.

The choice among these three strategies—and the skill to make that choice correctly—is a core competency for AI engineers. Most practical tasks today can be addressed through some combination of prompt engineering and RAG. Fine-tuning is reserved for cases where the other approaches fall short. Development from scratch—training a new model architecture on new data—is reserved for truly novel problems, typically involving scientific data or specialised domains where no existing foundation model has adequate coverage.

三、The Landscape of Generalisation

A recurring question in machine learning is whether a model can generalise—whether it can produce correct outputs for situations it has never encountered during training. In the era of foundation models trained on internet-scale data, this question takes on a different character. These models have been trained on such vast and diverse datasets that the set of situations they have encountered is extraordinarily broad. For most tasks that practitioners and users encounter daily, the model has likely seen something similar during training.

This is precisely why modern AI systems feel like general intelligence to many users: the tasks we perform are, in a real sense, within the distribution of the model's training data. The model is not extrapolating to genuinely novel situations; it is interpolating within an enormously rich training distribution. True generalisation—the ability to perform well on tasks that are fundamentally unlike anything in the training data—remains an open research challenge, most relevant in scientific domains and at the frontiers of human knowledge.

四、Summary

Foundation models are created through a two-phase pipeline. Pre-training on massive datasets through generic tasks like next-token prediction produces a base model with broad knowledge and linguistic competency but limited practical utility. Post-training through instruction fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback transforms this base into a model that can follow instructions, answer questions, generate code, and interact helpfully with users. Practitioners adapt these models to specific tasks through three strategies of increasing cost and complexity: prompt engineering and context engineering, retrieval-augmented generation, and fine-tuning. The choice among strategies, and the judgement to make that choice well, is the domain of the AI engineer—and it depends directly on the input-output analysis and modality characterisation developed in the preceding chapters.

Chapter 5: Agentic AI and Conversational Commerce

一、Introduction and Overview

The preceding chapters established what AI is, how to frame problems in input-output terms, and how generative models are built and adapted. This chapter brings those ideas together in the context of agentic AI—systems that independently pursue goals, make decisions, and take actions within their domain of expertise. Through the lens of conversational commerce, a rapidly emerging paradigm in customer engagement, this chapter examines how autonomous AI agents are designed, what architectural components they require, and how the principles of solution architecture apply to building systems that interact with real users in real time.

二、Core Concepts and Principles

1. What Makes a System Agentic

An agent is a computer-controlled program that interacts with its surroundings and takes autonomous actions on its own. Three properties distinguish an agentic AI system from a simple model endpoint.

The first is state awareness. The agent must perceive and maintain an understanding of the current situation—who the user is, what they have said, what has happened so far in the conversation, and what relevant information exists in external systems. A barista bot that remembers a customer's usual pickup branch is demonstrating state awareness.

The second is the ability to take reactive and proactive actions. Reactive actions respond to explicit user requests: the customer asks for a cappuccino, and the bot processes the order. Proactive actions anticipate needs or pursue goals that the user has not explicitly requested: the bot suggests a trending Belgian chocolate variant (upselling) or offers a masala cheese toast alongside the coffee (cross-selling). The decision of when to be proactive, how aggressively to pursue secondary goals, and when to stop pushing—all of these require judgement that must be specified in the system's design.

The third is access to external tools, knowledge, and data. An agent that can only generate text from its training knowledge is severely limited. A useful agent can query databases (to look up order history), call APIs (to check inventory or process payments), search knowledge bases (to find trending products or company policies), and invoke specialised models (to analyse uploaded images for quality issues).

2. Agent Architecture: Goals, Tools, and Sub-Agents

An agentic system operates within a defined architecture. At its centre is the AI agent itself—a language model with a system prompt that defines its goals, personality, constraints, and available tools. Surrounding it are the components that give it capability.

Goal descriptions tell the agent what it is trying to achieve. For a barista bot, the goals might be: maximise revenue per customer, maintain a positive and efficient customer experience, and never misrepresent products or prices. For a customer support agent, the goals might be: resolve the customer's issue as quickly as possible, escalate to a human when confidence is low, and collect structured feedback after resolution.

Tool descriptions tell the agent what external systems it can interact with and how to invoke them. A tool might be a SQL query that retrieves customer order history, an API call that checks inventory at a specific branch, a payment gateway that generates a payment link, or a computer vision model that analyses uploaded images. Each tool has a defined interface: what inputs it accepts, what outputs it produces, and what conditions should trigger its use.

Sub-agents extend the architecture beyond a single monolithic agent. Instead of one agent handling everything—upselling, cross-selling, tool invocation, conversation management—the primary agent can delegate to specialised sub-agents that run in parallel. An upselling sub-agent analyses the current conversation and suggests premium alternatives. A cross-selling sub-agent identifies complementary products. A trend analysis sub-agent checks what is popular in the customer's area. Each sub-agent processes the conversation context and returns its recommendation; the primary agent then synthesises these inputs and decides what to say.

Sub-agents offer practical advantages. They reduce the time to first token because multiple analyses can run simultaneously. They improve modularity: the upselling logic can be updated independently of the cross-selling logic. And they make the system easier to test: each sub-agent can be evaluated in isolation.

3. Model Context Protocol and Tool Integration

The mechanism by which agents interact with external tools is formalised through protocols like the Model Context Protocol (MCP). MCP defines how the AI agent discovers available tools, invokes them with appropriate parameters, and processes their responses. The agent's system prompt includes descriptions of each tool—what it does, what parameters it accepts, what it returns—and the language model learns to generate structured tool invocations when it determines that external information or action is needed.

This tool-use capability is introduced during post-training. Foundation models are specifically trained to recognise when a tool call is appropriate, format the call correctly, and integrate the tool's response into its ongoing generation. The quality of tool use varies across models and improves with each generation of post-training.

三、Practical Case Studies

1. The Automated Barista

An automated barista bot illustrates the full spectrum of agentic capabilities in a conversational commerce context. When a customer initiates a conversation with "I want to order a cappuccino," the bot identifies the intent (order placement) and the product (hot cappuccino). Rather than immediately processing the order, the bot's prompt instructs it to attempt upselling: it mentions a Belgian chocolate variant that is trending at the customer's local branch, at a higher price point.

The customer declines. The bot makes a softer upsell attempt—offering alternative milk options (oat, soy) that carry a slight premium. The customer declines again. The bot gracefully abandons upselling and proceeds to process the regular hot cappuccino with regular milk. The decision of when to stop upselling—after two attempts rather than five—is a specification choice that reflects taste and customer experience priorities. Too aggressive, and customers abandon the conversation. Too passive, and revenue opportunities are missed.

Having concluded the upsell phase, the bot transitions to personalisation. Drawing on the customer's order history stored in an external database, it suggests the HSR branch for pickup—the branch the customer has used before. This requires a tool call: querying the customer database, retrieving the most recent pickup location, and incorporating that information into the response. The customer confirms.

The bot then attempts cross-selling: "Would you like to add a masala cheese toast?" This is a different goal from upselling—selling a complementary product rather than a premium version of the original. The customer declines, and the bot moves to checkout: total price, estimated preparation time, and a payment link sent through the chat.

Behind this seemingly simple conversation, four different databases are being accessed: customer order history, branch staff schedules, regional trend data, and the product menu with current pricing. Multiple decisions are being made autonomously at each turn. The entire interaction feels like a conversation with a knowledgeable human barista, but it requires no human labour beyond the initial specification and ongoing monitoring.

2. AI-Powered Customer Support

A customer support system for an e-commerce platform like Swiggy or Amazon illustrates a more complex agentic workflow. The customer submits a query: "I received rotten apples." The system's first task is intent detection—classifying the query as a complaint about product quality rather than a delivery timing issue, a payment problem, or a general enquiry.

Intent detection is itself a decision point. If the query is simple—"Where is my order?"—the system executes a direct tool call to the delivery tracking API and returns the result. No multi-step reasoning is required. If the query is complex—as with the rotten apples—it enters an AI-assisted agent workflow.

The AI-assisted agent must now gather additional information. How many apples were in the order? Were all of them rotten, or only some? This requires a multi-turn conversation with the customer. Simultaneously, a sub-agent checks the customer's return history. If this customer has a pattern of returning products at high frequency, the system treats the complaint with additional scrutiny—potentially requiring photographic evidence before issuing a refund.

If an image is uploaded, a computer vision model (which might be a lightweight classifier rather than a full transformer, matching the tool to the task) evaluates whether the image indeed shows rotten produce. The result feeds back into the agent's decision-making.

Throughout this process, guardrails are in place. If the agent's confidence in its assessment drops below a threshold, or if the customer expresses frustration beyond a certain level, the system escalates to a human agent. The fallback to human intervention is not a failure mode but a designed feature—an acknowledgement that AI systems have limits and that certain situations require human judgement.

The entire workflow, from query to resolution, can be specified in natural language and implemented through a coding agent. What cannot be automated is the architectural thinking that determines the workflow in the first place: deciding which paths exist, where tool calls are needed, what the escalation criteria should be, and how to balance efficiency with customer satisfaction.

3. Conversational Commerce for Small Businesses

Beyond enterprise applications, conversational commerce represents a significant opportunity for small businesses. A neighbourhood butter seller who takes orders through WhatsApp is already engaging in conversational commerce—but with all the overhead falling on the human operator. Mapping out every step of that interaction and replacing the human backend with an AI agent creates a scalable system: the Kirana store owner can reach more customers in the locality without proportionally increasing labour costs.

The business opportunity is substantial. Customer preference for messaging over calling continues to grow. Conversion rates from conversational interactions are significantly higher than from traditional e-commerce interfaces. Instagram-based and WhatsApp-based commerce, where purchases happen through conversations rather than through menu-based navigation, is an emerging paradigm that aligns perfectly with agentic AI capabilities.

Building such a system requires connecting multiple components: a messaging platform (WhatsApp Business API), an inventory management backend (potentially as simple as a spreadsheet for a Kirana store), a payment integration layer, and an AI agent that manages the conversation. Each component has a well-defined role, and the AI agent serves as the orchestration layer that ties them together.

四、Design Principles for Agentic Systems

Several principles emerge from these case studies that apply broadly to agentic AI system design.

Simpler solutions are better. The fewer moving parts a system has, the easier it is to maintain, debug, and serve. A solution that handles 99% of use cases with three decision points is preferable to one that handles 99.5% with fifteen decision points, because the marginal improvement rarely justifies the added complexity.

Specifications must encode taste. When to upsell and when to stop. When to ask for photographic evidence and when to trust the customer. When to escalate to a human and when to persist. These are not technical decisions that a model can derive from first principles; they are design choices that reflect business goals, customer expectations, and domain expertise. They must be articulated explicitly in the system specification, because the AI agent will execute exactly what is specified—no more, no less.

The unique value of the human architect lies in context. Given a blank prompt asking for "a customer support system," any language model will produce a generic solution. The architect's value comes from knowing this specific business, this specific customer base, these specific failure modes, and these specific success metrics. That contextual knowledge, translated into precise specifications, is what produces systems worth paying for.

五、Summary

Agentic AI systems pursue goals autonomously through state awareness, reactive and proactive actions, and access to external tools. Their architecture combines a central agent with goal descriptions, tool descriptions, and optional sub-agents that run in parallel. Conversational commerce—from automated baristas to customer support to small-business messaging—provides concrete examples of how these systems create value by replacing human labour in repetitive conversational interactions while maintaining (or exceeding) the quality of human engagement. The design of these systems requires not just technical knowledge but taste: the ability to specify the right level of complexity, the right balance of autonomy and escalation, and the right goals for each interaction.

Chapter 6: Specification-Driven Development and the AI/ML Workflow

一、Introduction and Overview

The previous chapters established what AI is, how to frame problems, how generative models work, and how agentic systems are designed. This final chapter addresses the practical question that ties everything together: how do you actually build these systems? The answer, in 2026, is not "learn to code"—at least not in the traditional sense. It is specification-driven development (SDD): a disciplined methodology in which the human architect writes precise specifications and an AI coding agent handles implementation. This chapter contrasts SDD with ad-hoc "vibe coding," introduces the six-step AI/ML workflow that structures the entire development lifecycle, and establishes the architect-contractor mental model that defines the practitioner's role.

二、Core Concepts and Principles

1. The Problem with Vibe Coding

Ad-hoc prompting of AI coding tools—colloquially known as "vibe coding"—follows a predictable pattern. The developer writes a prompt, receives generated code, runs it, discovers it does not work, writes another prompt to patch the problem, receives more code, patches again, and eventually arrives at a hodgepodge of code that appears to function. There is no shared source of truth, no documented design intent, no test coverage, and no clear direction.

This approach has several failure modes. After enough turns, the coding agent's context window fills up or drifts, and it loses track of the original intent. The generated code is difficult to review because there is no specification against which to evaluate it. It is nearly impossible to hand over to another person—or even to one's future self. Returning to a vibe-coded project after a few days away means confronting code whose logic is opaque and whose generating prompts are scattered across a conversation history.

Vibe coding may suffice for fifty-line scripts or throwaway experiments. It categorically fails for systems that people will pay for, rely on, or need to maintain over time. The distinction is not about skill level but about methodology: vibe coding is structurally incapable of producing reliable, reviewable, maintainable software at scale.

2. Specification-Driven Development

Specification-driven development inverts the vibe coding workflow. Instead of starting with a prompt and hoping for useful code, SDD starts with a specification—a precise document that states what the system should do, how success is measured, and what tests must pass.

The specification is the single source of truth. It describes the task in concrete terms: the input format, the expected output format, the edge cases that must be handled, the performance requirements, the success metrics, and the acceptance criteria. When the specification is complete, it is handed to an AI coding agent (such as Claude Code) as a contract to execute.

The agent then plans: step one, step two, step three, each derived from the specification. The human architect reviews and approves the plan. The agent implements the plan, and the human reviews changes through a diff—a focused view of what changed, not the entire codebase. The human verifies that tests pass and that the implementation meets the acceptance criteria defined in the specification.

If something needs to change, the specification is updated, not the code. The updated specification drives a new implementation cycle. This ensures that the source of truth is always the specification, not the code, and that every change is traceable to a deliberate design decision.

3. The Architect-Contractor Mental Model

SDD requires a shift in mental model. The practitioner is not a coder; they are an architect. Claude (or whichever AI coding agent is used) is not a collaborator; it is a contractor. The architect defines what must be built. The contractor builds it. The architect reviews the work. The contractor revises as directed.

This maps directly to traditional software development roles. The product manager writes the specification (the "what"). The engineering manager creates the plan (the "how"). The developers implement. The testing engineers verify. In SDD, the AI coding agent fills the roles of engineering manager, developer, and tester. The human retains the product manager's role—defining the specification—and adds the critical function of review: examining diffs, verifying test results, and ensuring that the output matches the intent.

This mental model has a practical metric: if you never run out of your context window, you are specifying things correctly. Running out of context is a signal that the specification is too vague, that too much ad-hoc prompting is happening, or that the work should be broken into smaller, well-specified units.

4. The Role of Taste

The shift from coding to specification-driven development elevates one human capability above all others: taste. Taste is the ability to look at a model's output and determine whether it is correct. It is the ability to anticipate failure modes before they occur and write specifications that prevent them. It is the ability to define success metrics that actually capture what matters to the business. It is the ability to know when a solution is good enough and when it needs further refinement.

Taste cannot be delegated to an AI because it requires domain knowledge, business context, and aesthetic judgement that are specific to the practitioner's situation. An AI agent can generate a slide deck, but it cannot deliver the presentation with the confidence and conviction that builds stakeholder trust. An AI agent can produce technically correct code, but it cannot determine whether the overall solution architecture serves the business need.

This is why presentation skills, storytelling with data, and stakeholder communication have become more important, not less, in the age of AI-assisted development. When the AI can generate the artifact, the human's value lies entirely in specifying the right artifact and communicating its significance. Several project presentations have demonstrated the failure mode: beautifully AI-generated slides paired with presenters who cannot explain what is on them, immediately destroying stakeholder trust.

三、The Six-Step AI/ML Workflow

1. Step One: Frame the AI Problem from the Business Need

Every AI project begins with a business need, not a model. "Reduce customer service response time." "Increase conversion rates in our online store." "Automate the intake process at our clinic." The business need is decomposed into sub-problems, and each sub-problem is evaluated through the three diagnostic questions: Does it require human training? Did behaviour emerge through learning? Does it involve autonomous decisions?

For each identified AI sub-problem, a current baseline is established—what is being done today, whether by humans or by existing software. Success metrics are defined: F1 score for classification, time to prediction, cost per query, customer satisfaction score. The success metrics must be quantitative and must reflect the actual business objective, not just the technical performance of a model.

This step is the province of the product manager or solution architect. It requires domain knowledge, customer understanding, and the ability to think systematically about a complex process. It produces a specification—the document that drives all subsequent steps.

2. Step Two: Gather and Prepare Data

Data must be collected for each AI sub-problem. This might involve querying a company's data lake, scraping public datasets, collecting new data through experiments or user interactions, or labelling existing data for supervised learning. Data collection can itself be an AI task—using models to label images, classify documents, or extract structured information from unstructured sources.

Data cleaning—handling missing values, correcting errors, normalising formats, removing duplicates—is typically the most time-consuming part of any AI project. With SDD, much of this work can be specified and delegated to a coding agent, but the specification must be precise: what constitutes a missing value, how duplicates are defined, what normalisation scheme to use.

3. Step Three: Explore Models and Develop Solutions

With clean data and a defined baseline, the practitioner explores the range of available models. This might include API calls to frontier models (Claude, GPT, Gemini), open-source models (Llama, Mistral), or classical ML models (XGBoost, random forests). For each option, the success metric is measured through a procedure called cross-validation, which will be covered in detail in subsequent technical sessions.

The exploration is structured: identify ten to twenty candidate approaches from literature review, industry benchmarks, and API documentation. Run each on the prepared data. Tabulate the results. This is research—systematic investigation to determine which approach best meets the specified success criteria.

In some cases, prompt engineering or context engineering will suffice. In others, fine-tuning an existing model on domain-specific data will be necessary. In rare cases—typically involving scientific data or highly specialised domains—development from scratch may be required. The space for new model development has narrowed considerably as foundation models have grown more capable, but it remains important at the frontiers of research.

4. Step Four: Assemble the Solution and Communicate Results

Individual sub-problem solutions are assembled into an end-to-end system. This integration step often reveals new challenges: components that work well in isolation may interact poorly, latency may compound across multiple API calls, and edge cases may emerge at the boundaries between components.

The assembled solution must be presented to stakeholders as a story supported by data. The tabulated metrics from the exploration phase, the comparison with the baseline, the identified limitations, and the recommended deployment plan all feed into a narrative that builds trust and justifies investment. This storytelling step is irreducible—no AI agent can substitute for a practitioner who understands the business context and can answer stakeholder questions with confidence.

5. Step Five: Deploy

Deployment involves moving the solution from a development environment into production. This requires attention to infrastructure (hosting, scaling, monitoring), operational practices (MLOps, LLMOps), security (access control, data privacy), and reliability (fallback mechanisms, error handling).

Deployment also introduces the concept of prompt and API history management. In a world where models are accessed through APIs and behaviour is shaped by prompts, practitioners must maintain version histories of prompts, model versions, and data snapshots, just as they previously maintained version histories of code and training data.

6. Step Six: Monitor and Retrain

Deployed systems must be monitored continuously. Has the distribution of incoming data changed? Have customer complaint patterns shifted? Is the model's accuracy degrading over time? These phenomena—collectively known as drift—require detection and response.

When drift is detected, the cycle repeats: new data is gathered, models are re-evaluated, specifications are updated, and the system is retrained or re-prompted. This monitoring loop is where taste becomes especially important: recognising that a success metric is being met technically but that the business objective is not being achieved, and having the insight to redefine the success metric accordingly.

四、The Changing Role of the Software Engineer

The six-step workflow, executed through specification-driven development, redefines the software engineer's role. The technical activities that previously required years of coding expertise—data manipulation with pandas, model training with PyTorch, API integration, deployment scripting—can now be specified in natural language and implemented by an AI coding agent.

What remains uniquely human is the work at the beginning and end of the pipeline: framing the problem, writing specifications, reviewing outputs, communicating with stakeholders, monitoring deployed systems, and making judgement calls about when to retrain, re-specify, or redesign. The skill set has shifted from syntax to semantics, from implementation to architecture, from coding to specification and evaluation.

This shift is already visible across the industry. Product managers are expected to understand technical implementation at a deeper level. Software engineers are expected to think like product managers about business impact and user experience. The roles are converging, and the common ground is the ability to specify clearly, evaluate rigorously, and communicate compellingly. These are the skills that this course aims to develop.

五、Summary

Specification-driven development replaces ad-hoc vibe coding with a disciplined methodology: write a specification, let an AI agent plan and implement, review diffs, verify tests, and iterate on the specification as needed. The six-step AI/ML workflow—frame the problem, gather data, explore models, assemble and communicate, deploy, and monitor—provides the lifecycle structure within which SDD operates. The practitioner's role is that of an architect, not a coder: defining what must be built, evaluating whether it was built correctly, and communicating its value to stakeholders. Taste—the ability to judge quality, anticipate failures, and make design decisions that reflect business reality—is the irreducible human contribution that no AI agent can replicate.

Chapter 7: Specification-Driven Development in Practice

一、Introduction and Overview

The previous chapter introduced specification-driven development as a methodology. This chapter makes it concrete. Working through the mechanics of Claude Code as an agentic coding environment, it examines how specifications are written, how the agent operates through its three-phase loop, how context and memory are managed, and what distinguishes a good specification from a bad one. Along the way, it addresses the practical realities of debugging in an SDD workflow, the concept of AI slop, and the boundaries of what specification-driven development can and cannot accomplish.

二、Core Concepts and Principles

1. The Agentic Coding Environment

An agentic coding environment differs from a chat interface in a fundamental way. A chat interface facilitates a multi-turn text exchange between a user and a language model—prompt in, response out, prompt in, response out. An agentic coding environment elevates this to goal-directed autonomous action. The user provides a goal (defined in a specification), the language model reasons and plans, and the system takes actions in the real environment—reading files, writing code, executing commands, searching codebases—in a continuous loop until the task is complete.

Claude Code embodies this architecture. When a user provides a task, the system enters a three-phase loop that repeats until completion. In the first phase, it gathers context: reading relevant files, searching the codebase with grep, checking the git state, and loading the CLAUDE.md memory file that contains standing instructions and project conventions. In the second phase, it takes action: editing files, creating new files, running bash commands, executing code, performing git operations, or spawning sub-agents for parallel work. In the third phase, it verifies results: checking its own work against the tests and acceptance criteria defined in the specification, and looping back if verification fails.

This gather-context, take-action, verify-results loop is the operational core of agentic coding. The user can interrupt at any point, and certain actions—particularly bash command execution and git operations—require explicit user approval unless overridden. This controlled autonomy ensures the agent works in alignment with the architect's intent while still operating independently enough to be productive.

2. The CLAUDE.md Memory File

The CLAUDE.md file is loaded into every session and serves as the agent's persistent memory. It contains standing instructions that the architect wants enforced across all interactions: coding conventions, documentation requirements, technology stack preferences, and behavioural guidelines like "in case of doubt, ask me" or "document all parts of the code with a minimal working example."

CLAUDE.md exists at three levels with cascading priority. A global CLAUDE.md in the user's home directory applies across all projects—this is where personal style preferences live. A project-level CLAUDE.md sits in the repository root and contains project-specific conventions, architecture summaries, and file structure documentation. A subdirectory-level CLAUDE.md can override or extend the project-level file for specific modules.

For new projects, the /init command scans the existing codebase and generates a CLAUDE.md with a structural summary of where to find things. This is not merely convenient—it is operationally critical. Without this structural summary, the agent must search (grep) for files every time it needs to make a change, consuming tokens that should be spent on reasoning and implementation. A well-maintained CLAUDE.md dramatically reduces token waste and keeps the agent focused on productive work.

The CLAUDE.md file should also be placed under version control. When multiple team members work on the same project, a shared CLAUDE.md ensures everyone's agent operates with the same context and conventions, producing consistent output across the team.

3. Anatomy of a Good Specification

The specification file (spec.md) is the single source of truth for any project. Five elements distinguish a specification that produces reliable results from one that leads to frustration and wasted tokens.

The first element is a clear, unambiguous goal stated in one sentence. "Fully connected neural network for MNIST digit classification" is clear. "Create a good website for e-commerce" is ambiguous. "Clean up my codebase" is ambiguous. "Make no mistakes" is both ambiguous and impossible. The goal must describe what the artifact does, not what you hope it achieves.

The second element is explicit constraints. What technology stack to use. What libraries are allowed and which are forbidden. What performance requirements must be met. What the architecture should look like. These constraints bound the solution space so the agent does not make arbitrary choices that conflict with the architect's intent.

The third element is precise input-output specification. The input format, shape, and size. The output format and structure. API signatures if applicable. For a machine learning project, this means specifying the data dimensions, the number of classes, the evaluation metric, and the success threshold.

The fourth element is measurable acceptance criteria. "Test accuracy must be greater than 0.95" is measurable. "The model should be really good" is not. Acceptance criteria must be quantitative and testable—they are what the agent uses to verify its own work. If the criteria cannot be evaluated programmatically, the verification loop breaks down.

The fifth element is an explicit list of output files. The agent must know exactly what artifacts to produce: which Python files, which configuration files, which documentation files. Without this list, the agent makes its own decisions about project structure, which may not align with the architect's expectations.

4. Debugging in an SDD Workflow

When something goes wrong in specification-driven development, the debugging process differs fundamentally from traditional coding. In traditional development, bugs typically reside in the implementation—a wrong variable name, a misplaced comma, an off-by-one error. In SDD, where the agent handles implementation, bugs more commonly originate in the specification or the acceptance criteria.

The diagnostic hierarchy runs as follows. First, check whether the specification correctly describes what you want. If the specification says one thing but you actually want another, updating the specification is the fix—not patching the code. Second, check whether the acceptance criteria correctly capture the intended behaviour. If the tests pass but the output is wrong, the tests are inadequate. Third, check whether the implementation correctly follows the specification. If the specification and tests are both correct but the output is still wrong, an implementation bug exists—but this is the least common case when working with a capable agent.

The crucial discipline is to never patch around failures at the code level. When a database query returns wrong results, the instinct is to fix that specific query. In SDD, the correct response is to check whether the specification correctly describes what should be queried, update the specification if needed, and let the agent re-implement from the corrected spec. This maintains the specification as the single source of truth and prevents the accumulation of ad-hoc patches that erode the codebase over time.

Version control is essential to this workflow. Every specification change, plan approval, and implementation cycle should be committed to git. If an implementation cycle goes wrong, the architect can rewind to the last good commit, update the specification, and re-implement cleanly rather than trying to patch a broken state. The ability to discard failed work and restart from a known good state—without losing weeks of developer time—is one of the most powerful advantages of SDD.

5. Context Management and Token Economy

Language models operate within a fixed context window—a maximum number of tokens that can be processed in a single session. Every file read, every search result, every prompt, and every response consumes tokens from this window. Managing context is therefore not an optional optimisation but a core competency.

Several practices keep context usage efficient. The /compact command summarises the conversation so far and frees context space, allowing work to continue without losing the thread. The /clear command wipes the conversation entirely for a fresh start. The /context command visualises current token usage, revealing where context is being consumed. The /rewind command restores the conversation to a previous point without affecting the code, useful for undoing exploratory prompts that bloated the context unnecessarily.

A well-specified project where the CLAUDE.md provides structural information and the spec.md provides clear requirements will consume far fewer tokens than one where the agent must search, explore, and guess. Running out of context is almost always a symptom of under-specified instructions or excessive ad-hoc prompting. The practical benchmark: if you never hit the context limit, your specifications are well-written.

6. Skills and Extensibility

Claude Code's behaviour can be extended through skills—reusable natural language instruction sets stored in the .claude/skills/ directory. A skill defines a name, a description, the tools it is allowed to use, auto-invocation keywords that trigger it from natural language, dynamic context it should gather before executing, and step-by-step task instructions.

A git commit skill, for example, might be configured to stage all changes, inspect the diff, generate a context-appropriate commit message referencing what changed, and commit—but never push automatically. The skill can be invoked explicitly with a slash command or triggered automatically when the user types natural language like "save my progress" or "checkpoint my work."

Skills embody a key principle of SDD: repeatable processes should be codified, not improvised. Just as the specification codifies what should be built, skills codify how recurring operations should be performed. Both reduce reliance on ad-hoc prompting and improve consistency across sessions.

三、The Concept of AI Slop

One of the most important concepts for practitioners to internalise is slop—output that an inexperienced person finds impressive but an experienced person recognises as garbage. Slop is the natural consequence of systems that are post-trained to make users happy rather than to be correct.

The mechanism is subtle. For well-known problems—like MNIST classification, standard web development patterns, or common data analysis tasks—the agent will produce correct output even from ambiguous specifications, because the correct approach is thoroughly represented in its training data. This creates a false sense of competence. When the practitioner moves to a genuinely novel problem—one where the correct approach is not in the training data—the agent will still produce output that looks professional. The formatting will be clean. The code will run without errors. The variable names will be sensible. But the substance may be fundamentally wrong: incorrect architecture choices, inappropriate loss functions, nonsensical hyperparameters.

An experiment illustrates the broader phenomenon. When presented with misinformation written in polished, well-formatted English, nearly 100% of participants believed it was true. When the same misinformation was presented in poor handwriting with spelling mistakes, nearly 100% recognised it as false. The content was identical; only the presentation changed. Language models produce beautifully formatted output by default, which triggers the same acceptance bias.

The antidote to slop is domain expertise—taste. The architect must know enough about the domain to recognise when the agent's output is correct and when it is plausible-looking nonsense. This is precisely why studying the fundamentals of deep learning, understanding what loss functions and optimisers do, and building intuition about model architectures remains essential even when the coding itself is delegated to an agent.

四、Where SDD Excels and Where It Struggles

Specification-driven development excels at greenfield projects with heavy boilerplate, refactoring of existing codebases, test generation, documentation, and any task where specifications can be written unambiguously and acceptance criteria can be verified programmatically. For the 98% of software engineering work that involves assembling known components in known patterns, SDD is transformatively productive.

SDD struggles with novel algorithmic contributions—new optimisation schemes, new neural architectures, deep mathematical derivations. It cannot reliably handle subtle numerical instabilities in scientific computing. In these domains, the agent is a contractor, not a researcher. It can implement a known algorithm from a clear specification, but it cannot invent a new algorithm or debug a numerical issue that requires deep mathematical insight.

The boundary maps directly to the earlier discussion of novelty. If a problem is new only to you or your company—but solved by the broader community—SDD works beautifully, because the solution exists in the agent's training data. If a problem is genuinely novel—new to the entire community—the agent will produce slop dressed in professional formatting. Recognising which category a problem falls into, and adjusting one's reliance on the agent accordingly, is a core judgement that every AI engineer must develop.

五、Summary

Specification-driven development in practice revolves around a disciplined workflow: write specifications and acceptance criteria, let the agent plan and implement through its gather-context, take-action, verify-results loop, review diffs, commit to version control, and iterate by evolving the specification rather than patching the code. The CLAUDE.md memory file, the spec.md specification, and git version control form the three pillars of this workflow. Context management through compacting, clearing, and rewinding keeps token usage efficient. Skills extend the agent's capabilities with reusable, codified procedures. And the concept of slop serves as a constant reminder that the agent's output must be evaluated with domain expertise, not accepted on the basis of professional formatting alone.

Chapter 8: Tokenization and the Unified View of AI Tasks

一、Introduction and Overview

The preceding chapters established that modern AI is powered by transformer models processing sequences of tokens. This chapter examines the crucial first step of that pipeline: how raw data—text, images, audio—is converted into the discrete tokens that transformers consume. It begins by demonstrating how classical computer vision and natural language processing tasks, once addressed by dozens of specialised models, have collapsed into a unified framework built on a single architecture. It then develops the technical details of byte pair encoding, the dominant tokenization algorithm for text, and contrasts it with the simpler patchification approach used for images.

二、The Unification of AI Tasks

1. From Task-Specific Models to Foundation Models

Between roughly 2012 and 2022, the dominant paradigm in computer vision was to train specialised models for specialised tasks. Image classification used one architecture (typically a convolutional neural network). Image segmentation—classifying every pixel as belonging to a particular class—used another (U-Net, for example). Object detection—drawing bounding boxes around objects and labelling them—used yet another (YOLO or Detection Transformer models). Each task had its own data format, its own evaluation metrics, its own state-of-the-art leaderboard, and its own body of research literature.

The same fragmentation characterised natural language processing. Spell checking was a rule-based dictionary lookup. Keyword-based information retrieval was an engineering problem solved by PageRank and its successors. Topic modelling and text classification used classical machine learning (Naive Bayes, support vector machines). Information extraction, text summarisation, and closed-domain conversation occupied a middle tier of difficulty. Machine translation, open-domain question answering, and general conversational agents sat at the top—the hardest problems, requiring the deepest understanding of language.

This landscape has been fundamentally reorganised. The insight driving the reorganisation is that every one of these tasks can be formulated as question answering. Image classification is answering "What is in this image?" Image segmentation is answering "What class does each pixel belong to?" Object detection is answering "Where are the objects and what are they?" Spell checking is answering "Are these words spelled correctly?" Text summarisation is answering "What is the essential content of this document?" Machine translation is answering "How would this text read in another language?"

When all tasks are question answering, all tasks can be handled by a single model that takes questions (in text) and produces answers (in text), potentially with visual or audio input alongside the text. That single model is the vision-language foundation model for tasks involving images, and the language foundation model for tasks involving only text. The transformer architecture serves both.

2. Practical Implications of Unification

This unification has profound practical consequences. Three years ago, teaching a deep learning course required covering convolutional neural networks for image tasks, recurrent networks for sequence tasks, U-Net for segmentation, YOLO for detection, BERT for text classification, and many more—each with its own architecture, training procedure, and deployment considerations. Today, understanding the transformer architecture and the tokenization pipeline that feeds it is sufficient to address virtually all of these tasks.

For practitioners, the implication is equally significant. When a new computer vision or NLP task arises, the first question is no longer "which specialised model should I use?" Cloud Code—or any capable coding agent—already knows that YOLO is state of the art for object detection, that U-Net variants handle segmentation well, and that specific transformer models excel at specific text tasks. The practitioner's role is to describe the task clearly in a specification; the agent handles model selection and implementation.

The deeper shift is from maintaining many specialised models—each requiring separate training pipelines, data formats, and monitoring—to maintaining a single vision-language model accessed through an API. The cost and complexity of managing dozens of models, each with its own drift characteristics and retraining schedule, far exceeds the cost of API calls to a foundation model, even if individual inferences are somewhat slower. This is a software engineering decision, not a machine learning decision, and it is driven by the same principles of simplicity and maintainability that guide all good system design.

3. The Model Does Not "Understand"

A persistent source of confusion is the language used to describe what models do. When we say a model "understands" an image or "recognises" objects, we are anthropomorphising. The model is a pattern-matching system—a parameterised function that takes numerical input and produces numerical output. It does not "try to understand" anything. It was trained on millions or billions of examples of input-output pairs, and it learned statistical patterns that allow it to produce useful outputs for new inputs that resemble its training data.

This distinction matters practically because it sets expectations correctly. A model that has been trained on billions of image-caption pairs can produce captions for new images that are statistically similar to its training data. It will fail on images that are genuinely unlike anything in its training distribution—not because it "doesn't understand" them, but because its pattern-matching has no relevant patterns to draw on. The same principle applies to all AI tasks: performance is bounded by the diversity and quality of the training data.

三、Text Tokenization: Byte Pair Encoding

1. The Need for Subword Tokenization

Text must be converted to numbers before a model can process it. The question is: at what granularity? Three options present themselves.

Character-level tokenization splits text into individual characters. This produces a very small vocabulary (256 characters for UTF-8 encoding) but very long sequences, since each word becomes multiple tokens. More importantly, individual characters carry little semantic information—the letter "t" in isolation tells the model almost nothing useful.

Word-level tokenization keeps each word as a single token. This preserves semantic meaning but creates an enormous vocabulary (hundreds of thousands of entries) and cannot handle words the model has never seen—every misspelling, neologism, or rare technical term becomes an unknown token.

Subword tokenization occupies the middle ground. Words are broken into meaningful parts—prefixes, stems, suffixes—that carry semantic and syntactic information. The word "tokenization" might be split into "token" and "ization," both of which the model has seen in other contexts. The word "newest" might remain whole if it appears frequently enough in the training data, or be split into "new" and "est" if it does not. This approach handles rare and novel words gracefully (by decomposing them into known parts) while keeping sequences reasonably short.

Human reading behaviour supports the subword approach. When encountering an unfamiliar word, humans do not process it character by character. They recognise familiar subword patterns—"pre-," "un-," "-tion," "-ment"—and use those patterns to infer meaning. Children demonstrate this naturally: encountering "presentation" and knowing that "present" has an opposite "absent," a child might coin "absentation"—a word that does not exist but reveals subword-level processing. Byte pair encoding formalises this intuition algorithmically.

2. The Byte Pair Encoding Algorithm

Byte pair encoding (BPE) builds a subword vocabulary from a training corpus through a simple iterative process.

The algorithm begins with a base vocabulary consisting of all 256 UTF-8 byte characters—every letter, digit, punctuation mark, and space that can appear in text. This base vocabulary can represent any text, character by character, but inefficiently.

The algorithm then examines the training corpus and counts every adjacent pair of tokens (initially, every adjacent pair of characters). The most frequently occurring pair is identified and merged into a single new token, which is added to the vocabulary. A merge rule is recorded: whenever these two characters appear adjacent, they should be merged.

This process repeats. After each merge, the corpus is re-scanned with the updated vocabulary, and the next most frequent pair is identified and merged. Each iteration adds one new token to the vocabulary and one new merge rule to the list. The process continues for a predetermined number of iterations—50,000 merges for GPT-2's vocabulary (producing a total vocabulary of 50,256 tokens: 256 base characters plus 50,000 merged tokens) or 200,000 merges for GPT-4's vocabulary.

To illustrate: suppose a training corpus contains the words "low" (5 times), "lower" (2 times), and "newest" (6 times). The initial vocabulary contains the individual characters {d, e, l, n, o, r, s, t, w, ·}. Counting character pairs across the corpus reveals that "w" and "e" appear adjacent most frequently (8 times, from "lower" and "newest"). The pair is merged into a new token "we," and the merge rule "w + e → we" is recorded. In the next iteration, "l" and "o" form the most frequent pair (7 times), producing the token "lo" and the merge rule "l + o → lo." Subsequent iterations merge "ne," "newe," "newes," and "newest," each time adding a token and a rule.

3. Applying the Vocabulary

Once the vocabulary and merge rules are learned, tokenizing new text is straightforward. The input text is split into individual characters, then the merge rules are applied in the order they were learned. Each rule checks whether its pair of tokens appears adjacent in the current sequence and, if so, merges them.

For example, given the input "low newest" and the merge rules learned above, the process unfolds as follows. The initial character split produces [l, o, w, ·, n, e, w, e, s, t]. Applying the first merge rule (w + e → we) produces [l, o, we, ·, n, e, we, s, t]—but wait, only adjacent pairs in the current state are merged, so the process is more nuanced. The rules are applied sequentially, and each application may create new adjacent pairs that subsequent rules can merge. After all rules have been applied, the remaining tokens are the final tokenized representation.

Each token in the vocabulary is assigned a unique integer ID. The tokenized text is therefore a sequence of integers that the transformer model can process. The full pipeline runs: raw text → character split → sequential application of merge rules → sequence of token IDs → input to transformer.

4. Token Fertility and Multilingual Considerations

The vocabulary is built from a training corpus, and the composition of that corpus determines which languages are well-represented. If the corpus is predominantly English, English words and subwords will dominate the vocabulary, and English text will tokenize efficiently—common words consumed in one or two tokens. Text in Hindi, Tamil, or Kannada will tokenize less efficiently, requiring more tokens to represent the same semantic content, because the subword patterns of these languages are underrepresented in the vocabulary.

This phenomenon is called token fertility—the number of tokens required to represent a given amount of content in a particular language. High token fertility means the language is poorly served by the tokenization scheme: more tokens per concept means higher API costs, larger context consumption, and potentially degraded model performance.

Token fertility is why there is scope for language-specific model development even when the underlying transformer architecture is universal. A model with a vocabulary optimised for Kannada will tokenize Kannada text more efficiently than GPT-4's vocabulary, which must allocate its 200,000 tokens across all world languages. Companies developing Indic language models—like Sarvam or BharatGPT—differentiate themselves not through novel architectures but through tokenization schemes optimised for their target languages.

The core insight is that tokenization is not a neutral preprocessing step. It is a design decision that affects model performance, cost, and fairness across languages. The algorithm (BPE) is fixed; the variable is the training corpus from which the vocabulary is built.

四、Image Tokenization: Patchification

Image tokenization is conceptually simpler than text tokenization because images are already numerical. Each pixel has defined values (0-255 for each RGB channel), so no vocabulary needs to be built. The question is how to group pixels into tokens.

The dominant approach is patchification: dividing the image into non-overlapping square patches using a sliding window. A 224×224 image divided into 16×16 patches produces 196 patches, each containing 16 × 16 × 3 = 768 values (for RGB images). Each patch becomes one token—a vector of 768 numbers that captures the visual content of that region.

Patches can also be processed before being used as tokens. A small convolutional network might extract features from each patch, producing a more compact and informative token representation. However, the most common approach uses raw patches directly, letting the transformer model learn to extract relevant features during training.

For scientific data, alternative tokenization schemes become relevant. Fourier transforms, wavelet decompositions, or domain-specific representations might be more appropriate than raw spatial patches. These require building specialised training pipelines and training models from scratch on the new tokenization scheme—one of the cases where specification-driven development with existing foundation models falls short and genuine research contribution is needed.

五、The Complete Pipeline

With tokenization understood, the full pipeline from raw input to model processing becomes clear. Text arrives as a string of characters, is tokenized into subword tokens via byte pair encoding, and each token is mapped to its integer ID in the vocabulary. Images arrive as arrays of pixel values, are divided into patches, and each patch becomes a token vector. Audio is processed through its own tokenization scheme (spectrograms or learned audio tokens). All modalities are converted to sequences of tokens that the transformer processes uniformly.

The transformer architecture itself—the attention mechanism, the feedforward layers, the positional encodings—operates on these token sequences without knowing or caring whether the tokens originated from text, images, or audio. This modality-agnostic processing is what enables a single architecture to handle classification, segmentation, detection, translation, summarisation, question answering, code generation, and every other task discussed throughout this course.

The next step—which subsequent lectures will address in detail—is how the transformer processes these token sequences: the attention mechanism that allows each token to attend to every other token, the feedforward layers that transform token representations, and the training procedures that adjust the model's parameters to produce useful outputs.

六、Summary

The field of AI has undergone a remarkable consolidation. Dozens of specialised models for dozens of specialised tasks have been replaced by a unified framework: tokenize the input (using byte pair encoding for text, patchification for images), feed the token sequence to a transformer, and generate the output as a sequence of tokens. Byte pair encoding builds a subword vocabulary through iterative pair merging, producing a tokenization scheme that balances vocabulary size against sequence length and handles novel words gracefully. The choice of training corpus for BPE determines which languages are well-served, making tokenization a design decision with significant implications for cost, performance, and linguistic equity. Understanding tokenization is not merely a technical prerequisite for understanding transformers—it is a window into the design choices that shape the capabilities and limitations of every AI system built on the transformer architecture.

Chapter 9: Training Neural Networks — From Neurons to Gradient Descent

一、Introduction and Overview

The previous chapters established that modern AI is powered by transformer models processing token sequences. But how does a transformer—or any neural network—learn to produce useful outputs? This chapter answers that question by building up from the smallest unit of computation (the neuron) through the training procedure that estimates millions or billions of parameters. It introduces the critical distinction between parameters and hyperparameters, establishes the development-testing paradigm that safeguards against overfitting, and develops gradient descent through a vivid physical analogy that makes the optimisation process intuitive and memorable.

二、The Abstractions of a Neural Network

1. The Neuron

A neural network is built from three nested abstractions: neurons, layers, and networks. The neuron is the fundamental unit. Each neuron has exactly two jobs. First, it computes a linear combination of its inputs—multiplying each incoming signal by a weight, summing the results, and adding a bias term. Second, it applies a non-linear activation function to this sum, producing an output that is passed downstream.

The weights and biases are the properties of the neuron. They are the learnable quantities—the parameters that must be estimated from data during training. A parameter is not the data that flows through the network; it is the internal setting that determines how the network transforms that data. This distinction is fundamental: learning means estimating these parameters.

2. Layers and Networks

Multiple neurons arranged together form a layer. In a dense (fully connected) layer, every neuron is connected to every neuron in the preceding layer. This is what "dense" means: the connections are maximally dense. Other layer types—convolutional layers, attention layers—have different connection patterns, but the principle is the same: each layer transforms its input into a new representation.

Multiple layers stacked together form a network. The depth (number of layers) and width (number of neurons per layer) together determine the network's capacity—its ability to learn complex representations.

3. Calculating Parameters

The number of parameters in a dense layer can be calculated precisely. If a layer has N neurons and the preceding layer has M neurons, the weight matrix has N × M entries (one weight per connection), plus N biases (one per neuron), for a total of N × M + N parameters.

For example, an MNIST classifier might have an input layer of 784 neurons (a 28×28 image flattened), a hidden layer of 100 neurons, a second hidden layer of 50, a third of 20, and an output layer of 10. The parameter count for each layer is: 100 × 784 + 100 = 78,500 for the first hidden layer; 50 × 100 + 50 = 5,050 for the second; 20 × 50 + 20 = 1,020 for the third; and 10 × 20 + 10 = 210 for the output layer. The total is 84,780 parameters. When people say a model has "70 billion parameters," they mean 70 billion weights and biases distributed across all layers.

三、Representation Learning

1. What Layers Actually Do

Geometrically, each layer performs a rotation and translation of the data in some vector space. The weights determine the rotation; the biases determine the translation. The dimension of that space is the width of the layer. Layer by layer, the data is transformed from its original representation (pixel values for images, token embeddings for text) into progressively more useful representations for the task at hand.

A compelling visual metaphor captures the essence of deep learning: imagine the input data as a crumpled ball of paper. Each layer partially uncrumples that ball, smoothing out the complex folds and creases until, at the final layer, the paper lies flat and the classification or prediction task becomes trivially easy. The entire quest of deep learning is to find these uncrumpling transformations—the representations that make downstream tasks simple.

2. Why Classification Matters for Generative AI

Classification might seem distant from the generation tasks that define modern AI, but it is in fact the core operation at every step of autoregressive generation. When a language model predicts the next token, it performs a multi-class classification over the entire vocabulary. Given the input sequence "IASc is located in," the model must classify the next token as "Bangalore" from a vocabulary of 200,000 options. Every step of the autoregressive loop is a classification problem. Understanding classification deeply is therefore essential for understanding how language models generate text.

四、The Training Procedure

1. Loss Functions

Training a neural network requires a quantitative measure of how wrong the model's predictions are. This measure is the loss function. For classification tasks, the standard loss function is cross-entropy, defined as the negative sum, over all classes, of the true label (in one-hot encoding) multiplied by the log-probability predicted by the model. Cross-entropy reaches its minimum (zero) when the model's predictions perfectly match the true labels and increases as predictions diverge from truth.

The softmax activation function converts the raw output of the final layer (logits) into probabilities that sum to one, enabling the cross-entropy calculation. For regression tasks—predicting continuous numbers rather than categories—the standard loss function is mean squared error: the average of the squared differences between predictions and true values.

2. Gradient Descent: The Nandi Hills Analogy

The optimisation algorithm that finds the best parameters is gradient descent, and it can be understood through a vivid physical analogy.

Imagine you are hiking on Nandi Hills near Bangalore. You have reached the peak, but suddenly pitch darkness falls—no light, no GPS, no visibility. Your car is parked in the valley below, and you need to reach it. Your only sensory input is the feeling of the ground beneath your feet.

The strategy is simple. You rotate your foot 360 degrees, feeling the slope in every direction. You identify the direction of steepest descent—the direction where the ground drops away most steeply. You take one small, measured step in that direction. Then you repeat: feel the slope, find the steepest descent, take another small step. You continue until your foot feels completely flat ground in all directions, indicating you have reached the valley.

Mathematically, this is gradient descent. The longitude and latitude of your position correspond to the model's parameters (weights W₁, W₂, ...). The altitude corresponds to the loss function. The gradient—the partial derivative of the loss with respect to each parameter—gives the direction of steepest ascent. Moving in the negative gradient direction gives steepest descent. The learning rate η is the size of each measured step.

The update rule is: W_new = W_old − η × ∂L/∂W, where L is the loss and ∂L/∂W is the gradient evaluated at the current parameter values.

3. Stochastic Gradient Descent and Non-Convex Landscapes

The Nandi Hills analogy works perfectly if the hill is smooth and convex—a single valley with no boulders, puddles, or false valleys along the way. If the landscape were convex, gradient descent would be guaranteed to find the unique global minimum from any starting point.

Neural network loss landscapes are emphatically not convex. They are riddled with local minima, saddle points, and flat regions. The solution is stochastic gradient descent (SGD): instead of computing the gradient on the entire training dataset, compute it on a random subset—a mini-batch.

Because each mini-batch yields a slightly different estimate of the gradient, the effective loss landscape changes subtly at every step. A puddle that would trap standard gradient descent becomes escapable when the landscape shifts beneath your feet. On average, across many mini-batches, the gradient points in the right direction, but the randomness provides the perturbation needed to escape local minima and saddle points.

An iteration is one parameter update using one mini-batch. An epoch is a complete pass through the entire training dataset—enough iterations to ensure every training example has been used at least once.

五、The Development-Testing Paradigm

1. Why Train-Test Is Not Enough

The most critical lesson in machine learning methodology is the development-testing paradigm. The entire dataset is split into a development set and a test set. The development set is further split into training and validation subsets. The test set is never used for training or hyperparameter tuning—its sanctity is paramount.

Training uses the training subset to update parameters via gradient descent. Hyperparameter tuning uses the validation subset to evaluate different model configurations (depth, width, learning rate, activation functions) and select the best one. The test set is used only at the very end, on the final model, to estimate expected real-world performance.

If the test set performance is poor and you use that information to go back and tune hyperparameters, the test set is compromised. You have optimised for the test set, which means it no longer represents unseen data. You would need to obtain entirely new unseen data to get an honest performance estimate. This is called test set leakage, and it is the most common methodological error in applied machine learning.

2. Hyperparameters: Model and Algorithmic

Hyperparameters are the choices that must be made before training begins. They fall into two categories. Model hyperparameters define the architecture: number of layers, width of each layer, type of activation function, type of layer (dense, convolutional, attention). Algorithmic hyperparameters define the training process: learning rate, batch size, choice of optimiser (SGD, Adam, RMSProp), regularisation strength.

Parameters are learned through gradient descent. Hyperparameters are tuned through cross-validation on the development set. It is mathematically impossible to optimise parameters and hyperparameters simultaneously in a single optimisation loop—they must be handled in two nested steps.

六、Optimisation versus Learning

1. The Fundamental Distinction

The training loss curve always decreases—given enough capacity, a neural network can memorise every training example and drive the training loss to zero. But this is not the goal. A model that has memorised its training data perfectly will fail on new data it has never seen. This is overfitting.

The validation loss tells a different story. It decreases initially as the model learns genuine patterns, then begins to increase as the model starts memorising training-specific noise rather than learning generalisable patterns. The point where validation loss begins to rise is the sweet spot—the optimal point to stop training.

This is the fundamental distinction between optimisation and learning. Optimisation seeks the global minimum of the loss function. Learning seeks the parameter state that generalises best to unseen data. These are not the same thing. The global minimum of the training loss corresponds to total memorisation, which is counterproductive. The ideal stopping point is a local minimum—or not even a minimum at all—that happens to produce the best validation performance.

We are not interested in global minima. We are not even looking for minima. We are looking for the parameter state that ensures robust performance on data the model has never seen. Monitoring the validation curve and stopping training at the right moment is how this is achieved in practice.

七、Summary

A neural network is a deep stack of layers, each containing neurons that perform linear combinations (rotation and translation) followed by non-linear activations. The parameters—weights and biases—are estimated through training, which uses stochastic gradient descent to minimise a loss function (cross-entropy for classification, mean squared error for regression) computed on mini-batches of data. The development-testing paradigm protects against overfitting by separating data into training, validation, and test subsets, with the test set held inviolate until final evaluation. The critical insight is that learning is not optimisation: the goal is not the global minimum of the training loss but the parameter state that best generalises to unseen data, identified by monitoring the validation loss and stopping training at the right moment.

Chapter 10: The Transformer Architecture and Self-Attention

一、Introduction and Overview

Every chapter in this course has been building toward this one. The input-output mental model identified where AI is needed. The data modalities chapter catalogued what goes into models. The tokenization chapter showed how raw data becomes discrete tokens. The training chapter explained how parameters are learned. Now, at last, we examine the engine itself: the transformer architecture and the self-attention mechanism that powers it. The goal is a first-principles understanding deep enough that every step of the process—from token embeddings through attention computation to next-token prediction—can be explained from memory.

二、What the Transformer Must Do

A generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) is a language model: its job is to predict the probability of the next token given all preceding tokens. The input is a sequence of token embeddings—vectors picked from the vocabulary's embedding matrix. The output is a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary, from which the next token is selected.

Between input and output sits a stack of transformer blocks (decoders). These blocks must transform the sequence of token embeddings into a representation from which the next token can be correctly predicted. This is exactly the representation learning framework established in the previous chapter: the transformer blocks uncrumple the complex manifold of raw embeddings into a smooth space where classification (picking the right next token from 200,000 vocabulary entries) becomes tractable.

The core question is: what mechanism do these transformer blocks use to create useful representations? The answer is self-attention.

三、Self-Attention: The Core Mechanism

1. The Problem: Context-Agnostic Embeddings

When a token is looked up in the vocabulary, its embedding is always the same regardless of context. The word "station" gets the same vector whether it appears in "train station," "police station," or "space station." But meaning depends on context—the surrounding words determine what "station" actually refers to. The embedding must be modulated by context to become useful.

The goal of self-attention is to convert a sequence of context-agnostic token embeddings into a sequence of context-aware representations, where each token's vector has been adjusted to reflect its relationship with every other token in the sequence.

2. Level Zero: The Basic Intuition

Consider the sentence "she is eating a green apple." To understand this sentence, the model should recognise that the words "eating" and "apple" are highly relevant to each other—they form the core meaning. The word "green" modifies "apple" and is also relevant. But "eating" and "green" have little direct relationship.

The simplest mechanism to capture these relationships is to compute the dot product between every pair of token vectors. The dot product measures similarity: vectors pointing in similar directions produce high dot products; orthogonal vectors produce zero. If we compute dot products between all pairs in a sequence of length 7, we get a 7×7 matrix of relevance scores—the attention score matrix.

For each token, we extract its column of scores, convert them into weights (through normalisation), and compute a weighted sum of all token vectors in the sequence. The result is a new vector for that token that incorporates information from every other token, weighted by relevance. Repeating this for every token produces a complete sequence of context-aware representations.

3. The Missing Ingredient: Learnability

The level-zero mechanism has a critical flaw: there is nothing learnable in it. The dot products are computed directly on the input embeddings, and the weighted sum follows mechanically. No parameters are being estimated, which means the network cannot improve this process through training. Deep learning requires learnable weights and biases at every stage.

If the level-zero mechanism were inserted into a transformer block, the weight matrices WQ, WK, and WV would all be identity matrices—the inputs would pass through unchanged. The question becomes: how do we introduce learnable transformations that allow the attention mechanism to discover which relationships matter?

四、Query-Key-Value Attention

1. Inspiration from Search Engines

The solution comes from an analogy to database retrieval. In a search engine, a user provides a query ("dogs on the beach"). The database contains values (images) indexed by keys (labels like "beach," "dog," "tree"). The engine computes the match between the query and each key, then retrieves the values corresponding to the best-matching keys, ranked by match score.

Vaswani et al., working at Google in 2017, adapted this paradigm for self-attention. Every token embedding is projected through three separate dense layers to produce three vectors: a query vector Q (what this token is looking for), a key vector K (what this token offers to others), and a value vector V (the information this token contributes when attended to).

The learnable parameters are the weight matrices of these three dense layers: WQ, WK, and WV. These are the parameters that training adjusts through gradient descent. Everything else in the attention computation—the dot products, the scaling, the softmax, the weighted sum—is deterministic given Q, K, and V.

2. The Computation Step by Step

Given a sequence of token embeddings X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ, the attention mechanism proceeds as follows.

In the first step, every embedding is projected through three dense layers to produce query, key, and value vectors. X₁ becomes Q₁, K₁, V₁. X₂ becomes Q₂, K₂, V₂. The same weight matrices WQ, WK, and WV are applied to every token in the sequence—this weight sharing is what allows the mechanism to handle sequences of any length.

In the second step, attention scores are computed as dot products between queries and keys. For token i, the scores are Q_i · K₁, Q_i · K₂, ..., Q_i · Kₙ. This produces one score for every pair of tokens—measuring how much token i should attend to each other token.

In the third step, scores are scaled by dividing by the square root of the embedding dimension (√d). In the original transformer paper, d was 64, so the divisor was 8. This scaling is a heuristic that prevents the dot products from growing too large in magnitude, which would push the subsequent softmax into regions of near-zero gradient. The specific value is less important than the principle: raw dot products need normalisation.

In the fourth step, the scaled scores are passed through softmax, converting them into weights that are positive and sum to one. For token i, the softmax output gives the proportion of attention that token i should pay to each other token.

In the fifth step, the attention weights are used to compute a weighted sum of value vectors. The context-aware representation Z_i for token i is: Z_i = Σⱼ softmax(Q_i · Kⱼ / √d) × Vⱼ, where the sum runs over all positions j in the sequence.

The output is a complete sequence of context-aware vectors Z₁, Z₂, ..., Zₙ, each having the same dimensionality as the original embeddings but now encoding contextual information about the entire sequence.

3. The Attention Formula

The complete self-attention operation can be written compactly. For each position i in the sequence, the output is:

Z_i = Σⱼ softmax(Q_i · Kⱼ / √d) × Vⱼ

Or, in matrix notation for the entire sequence:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √d) × V

This single formula encapsulates the entire mechanism: project inputs to queries, keys, and values; compute scaled dot-product scores; normalise with softmax; take a weighted sum of values. The learnable parameters are WQ, WK, and WV—the dense layers that produce Q, K, and V from the input embeddings.

4. What Makes This Powerful

The power of self-attention comes from three properties. First, it is permutation-aware through the attention scores: every token can attend to every other token, capturing long-range dependencies that earlier architectures (recurrent networks, convolutional networks) struggled with. Second, it is learnable: the WQ, WK, WV matrices are adjusted during training so that the model learns which relationships between tokens matter for the task. Third, it is parallelisable: unlike recurrent networks that process tokens sequentially, all attention scores can be computed simultaneously, enabling efficient training on modern GPU hardware.

The self-attention mechanism is not the only component of a transformer block—there are also residual connections, layer normalisation, and feedforward (dense) layers that further process the attention outputs. Multiple attention heads operate in parallel, each with its own WQ, WK, WV matrices, allowing the model to attend to different types of relationships simultaneously. These details extend the basic mechanism but do not change its essence: self-attention converts context-agnostic embeddings into context-aware representations through learnable query-key-value projections and weighted summation.

五、Connecting to the Larger Picture

The transformer architecture achieves the representation learning goal that motivates all of deep learning. Raw token embeddings, which assign the same vector to "station" regardless of context, are transformed through multiple layers of self-attention into rich, context-aware representations where "station" in "train station" occupies a fundamentally different region of vector space than "station" in "police station."

These representations feed into the final classification layer—a dense layer with vocabulary-size output and softmax activation—that predicts the probability distribution over the next token. The cross-entropy loss between this prediction and the true next token provides the training signal. Gradient descent propagates this signal backward through all the transformer blocks, updating every WQ, WK, WV matrix, every dense layer weight, and every bias in the network.

Pre-training is this process at massive scale: trillions of next-token predictions across internet-scale data, adjusting billions of parameters until the model's representations capture the statistical structure of language deeply enough to predict continuations that humans find coherent and useful.

The transformer architecture has proven so effective that it has resisted replacement for nearly a decade. Its attention operations are being designed into silicon—tensor processing units and transformer accelerators bake these computations into hardware. The architecture is, for practical purposes, permanent. What varies is the data, the tokenization, the scale, and the post-training—the surrounding infrastructure that adapts the transformer's capabilities to specific tasks and domains.

六、Summary

The transformer architecture converts sequences of context-agnostic token embeddings into context-aware representations through self-attention, a mechanism in which every token attends to every other token via learnable query-key-value projections. The computation is: project embeddings to Q, K, V through dense layers; compute scaled dot-product scores Q·Kᵀ/√d; normalise with softmax; take a weighted sum of values. The learnable parameters—WQ, WK, WV—are what training adjusts through gradient descent on the cross-entropy loss of next-token prediction. This mechanism, repeated across multiple layers and multiple attention heads, enables the transformer to capture the contextual relationships in language (and other modalities) that make accurate generation possible. Understanding this mechanism from first principles—from the raw embedding lookup through the attention computation to the final softmax prediction—is the foundational technical competency for working with modern AI systems.

Chapter 11: The Complete GPT Pipeline — From Embedding to Token Generation

一、Introduction and Overview

The previous chapter introduced self-attention as the mechanism by which transformers create context-aware representations. This chapter assembles every component into the complete generative pre-trained transformer pipeline, from the moment tokens enter the model to the moment the next token is sampled and returned. It covers the engineering solutions that make deep training stable—skip connections and layer normalization—explains the language model head that converts hidden representations into vocabulary probabilities, and details the decoding strategies (temperature, top-K, top-P) that control how tokens are selected during generation.

二、The End-to-End Architecture

1. From Tokens to Embeddings

The pipeline begins with a sequence of token indices. Each index is mapped to a vector through the embedding matrix (vocabulary size × D_model), producing a sequence of token embeddings. Positional encodings—either learned or sinusoidal—are added element-wise, giving the model information about where each token sits in the sequence. The result is a matrix of shape (sequence_length × D_model) that serves as input to the first transformer block.

2. The Transformer Decoder Block

Each transformer block contains two main sub-layers. The first is masked multi-head self-attention: the input sequence is projected through WQ, WK, WV to produce queries, keys, and values, the D_model dimension is split across multiple attention heads (each head operating on D_model / num_heads dimensions), causal masking ensures each token attends only to preceding tokens, and the outputs from all heads are concatenated. The second sub-layer is a feedforward network: two dense layers with an activation function (GELU in GPT-2/3, SwiGLU in Llama and similar models) that further transform each token's representation independently.

The output of each transformer block has the same shape as its input—sequence_length × D_model—enabling blocks to be stacked. A GPT-2 model uses 12 blocks; larger models use 32, 64, or more. The total parameter count comes from summing the parameters of every dense layer across all blocks: the QKV projection layers inside each attention head, the concatenation projection, the feedforward layers, and the final language model head.

3. Skip Connections: Solving the Vanishing Gradient Problem

Training a deep stack of layers requires computing the gradient of the loss with respect to every parameter in every layer, propagated backward through the chain rule. If any intermediate gradient approaches zero—which happens easily with finite-precision arithmetic—all gradients for layers below that point also go to zero. Those parameters stop learning. This is the vanishing gradient problem.

The engineering solution is skip connections (residual connections). Instead of each block computing output = Block(input), the architecture computes output = input + Block(input). The block learns the residual—the difference between the desired output and the input—rather than the output directly. During backpropagation, the gradient flows through both the block and the skip connection. Even if the gradient through the block vanishes, the gradient through the skip connection provides an uninterrupted path to every layer, ensuring all parameters remain trainable.

Skip connections exist around both the attention sub-layer and the feedforward sub-layer within each transformer block. They are always active—in both forward and backward passes—and fundamentally change what each block learns: not the representation itself, but the adjustment to the representation.

4. Layer Normalization: Stabilising Activations

The second stability mechanism is layer normalization. After each sub-layer, the D_model-dimensional vector for each token is normalized to have zero mean and unit variance across its feature dimension. This prevents activations from exploding (growing unboundedly, since ReLU and GELU are not upper-bounded) or collapsing (shrinking toward zero), either of which would destabilise training.

The computation is straightforward: for each token's vector, calculate the mean and variance across the D_model entries, subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation, then apply a learned scale and bias shift. Layer normalization is applied per-token, across the feature dimension—in contrast to batch normalization (used in convolutional networks), which normalises across the batch dimension.

The historical origin is instructive. Batch normalization was proposed with a theory of "internal covariate shift" that its inventor later acknowledged was fabricated to satisfy peer reviewers—the technique worked empirically, and the theory was retrofitted. Layer normalization itself may have been discovered accidentally when an intern set the normalization axis to -1 instead of 1 in a Pytorch implementation. Much of deep learning is empirical engineering dressed in post-hoc theoretical justification.

Layer normalization can be applied before the sub-layer (pre-norm) or after it (post-norm), with pre-norm being more common in modern architectures. The choice affects training dynamics but not the fundamental principle: keep activations well-behaved so that gradients remain informative.

三、The Language Model Head

1. From Hidden State to Vocabulary Probabilities

After passing through all transformer blocks, the model holds a sequence of context-aware representations, each of dimension D_model. For next-token prediction, only the last token's representation matters—it encodes, through layers of attention, the accumulated context of the entire input sequence.

This D_model-dimensional vector is projected through a linear layer (the "unembedding matrix" or "de-tokenizer") to the vocabulary dimension, producing raw logits—one number per vocabulary entry. In GPT-2, this maps 768 dimensions to 50,257 vocabulary entries. In Llama, it maps 4,096 dimensions to a vocabulary of 32,000 or more.

The logits are unbounded real numbers. To convert them into a probability distribution, they are divided by a temperature parameter τ and passed through softmax. Temperature controls the sharpness of the distribution: τ = 1 gives the calibrated probabilities from training; τ < 1 makes the distribution sharper (concentrating probability on fewer tokens, producing more deterministic output); τ > 1 flattens the distribution (spreading probability more evenly, producing more diverse and creative output).

2. Sampling Strategies: Top-K and Top-P

With a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary, the model must select the next token. Two dominant strategies filter the distribution before sampling.

Top-K filtering sorts vocabulary entries by probability and retains only the K highest. The remaining entries' probabilities are renormalised, and the next token is sampled from this truncated distribution. K = 1 produces greedy decoding (always selecting the most probable token); larger K values allow more diversity.

Top-P (nucleus sampling) is dynamic: it retains the smallest set of vocabulary entries whose cumulative probability exceeds a threshold P (e.g., 0.85). When the model is confident (one token dominates), few entries are retained. When the model is uncertain, many entries pass the threshold. This adaptive behaviour often produces more natural-sounding text than fixed-K filtering.

The complete decoding pipeline is: produce logits → divide by temperature → apply top-K or top-P filtering → apply softmax → sample the next token. This token is appended to the input sequence, and the entire process repeats autoregressively.

四、Pre-Training as Next-Token Prediction

The entire architecture is trained end-to-end on a single task: predict the next token. A text corpus is decomposed into (input sequence, next token) pairs. The cross-entropy loss between the model's predicted probability distribution and the true next token provides the training signal. Gradients flow backward through the language model head, through every transformer block, through every attention head's QKV layers and feedforward layers, all the way to the embedding matrix.

This is why it is called generative pre-training: the model learns to generate by predicting what comes next, using general-purpose text data. No task-specific labels are needed—the text itself provides supervision. The pre-trained model captures the statistical structure of language: which tokens follow which, in what contexts, with what probabilities.

五、Summary

The complete GPT pipeline proceeds as follows: tokenize input text into indices; map indices to embeddings and add positional encodings; pass through N transformer decoder blocks, each containing masked multi-head self-attention and feedforward layers, stabilised by skip connections and layer normalization; extract the last token's D_model-dimensional hidden state; project through the language model head to vocabulary-dimensional logits; scale by temperature and filter with top-K or top-P; apply softmax to obtain probabilities; sample the next token. Every learnable parameter in this pipeline lives in a dense layer—the QKV projections, the feedforward networks, the embedding and unembedding matrices—and all are trained jointly through backpropagation on the cross-entropy loss of next-token prediction. Understanding this pipeline from end to end, being able to draw it and explain each component's role, is the core technical competency that the course aims to develop.

Chapter 12: Post-Training and Context Engineering

一、Introduction and Overview

A pre-trained transformer can predict the next token, but it cannot reliably follow instructions, provide accurate answers, or behave in ways that humans find useful. This chapter examines the two phases of post-training—supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback—that transform a base model into a capable assistant, and then introduces context engineering as the practitioner's primary mechanism for getting useful outputs without retraining: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and agentic patterns.

二、Post-Training: Aligning Models with Human Intent

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) uses task-specific labelled data to adjust the pre-trained model's weights and biases. The data consists of (prompt, correct response) pairs—for example, an input text paired with its human-written summary, or an instruction paired with the expected output. The same cross-entropy loss and gradient descent machinery used in pre-training is applied, but with a smaller learning rate and domain-specific data.

The economics of SFT are challenging. For a translation task, an expert who knows both languages might produce five high-quality translations per hour. At ₹500 per hour, each translation costs roughly ₹100. Scaling to 100,000 pairs—a modest dataset by language model standards—costs ₹1 crore. Quality control adds further expense: each translation must be verified, corrected, and validated. The human effort is the bottleneck, and it scales linearly with dataset size.

SFT produces models that follow instructions well within the distribution of training examples. But it cannot efficiently cover the full range of tasks that users will request, and the cost of comprehensive labelling is prohibitive.

2. Preference Fine-Tuning and RLHF

The insight behind reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) is that humans are better at comparing options than generating answers from scratch. Instead of asking a translator to write the correct translation (expensive), ask them to rate which of two machine-generated translations is better (cheaper). In the same time it takes to produce one translation, a human can rate four comparisons, reducing cost by roughly 75%.

The RLHF pipeline has three stages. In the first, the language model generates multiple responses to each prompt, using the stochastic sampling mechanisms (temperature, top-K, top-P) to produce diverse outputs. In the second, human evaluators rate or rank these responses—either on a scale or through pairwise comparison. Research has shown that pairwise comparison produces more reliable signals than absolute ratings: Netflix discovered this when switching from five-star ratings (where users became amateur critics and gave inconsistent scores) to simple thumbs-up/thumbs-down (which reliably captured preference).

In the third stage, a reward model is trained on the collected preference data. This is a simpler model—potentially a small bidirectional transformer—that takes a prompt-response pair and predicts the score a human would assign. The reward model replaces the human in the loop: it can evaluate thousands of responses per second at negligible cost.

With the reward model in place, reinforcement learning algorithms (PPO, DPO, GRPO) update the language model's weights to maximise the predicted reward. The language model generates responses, the reward model scores them, and the RL algorithm adjusts weights so that future responses score higher. The reward signal is sparse (one score per complete response, not per token) and noisy (the reward model is imperfect), but RL algorithms are designed to learn from exactly this kind of signal.

An alternative to learned reward models is verifiable rewards: deterministic systems that check whether a response is correct. For mathematics, the system can execute the proposed solution and compare against the known answer. For code generation, the system can run the code and verify test results. Verifiable rewards eliminate the noise of the reward model entirely, producing cleaner training signals for domains where correctness is objectively checkable.

3. Why RLHF, Not Just More SFT?

The question arises: if supervised fine-tuning already teaches the model to produce good outputs, why add reinforcement learning? The answer is economic and practical. SFT requires a human to generate the complete correct answer for every training example—expensive and slow. RLHF requires a human only to compare or rate model-generated answers—cheaper and faster. The RL algorithms can learn from noisy preference signals that would be insufficient for cross-entropy-based supervised training. The combination of cheaper data collection and algorithms that tolerate noise makes RLHF the dominant post-training paradigm for frontier models.

All post-training updates all parameters across all transformer layers, typically with a smaller learning rate than pre-training. The theoretical justification is that the pre-trained model occupies a region of the loss landscape that is approximately convex at small scales, so fine-tuning with small steps stays within this well-behaved region.

三、Context Engineering

1. Two Things a Model Needs

To solve any task, a language model needs instructions (how to do it) and context (the information needed to do it correctly). Writing good instructions is prompt engineering—or, in its more disciplined form, specification-driven development. Providing the right context is context engineering.

The distinction is illustrated by a simple task: "update my passbook." The instructions are generic—go to the bank, provide account details, ask the teller to print recent transactions. The context is specific—which bank, which branch, what account number, what identification. Without context, even perfect instructions produce no useful result.

Two dominant patterns provide context to language models: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and agentic patterns. Both were introduced conceptually earlier in the course; this chapter examines their technical implementation.

2. Retrieval-Augmented Generation

RAG adds context to a prompt by retrieving relevant information from an external memory—a document database, a knowledge base, chat history, or any structured or unstructured data source. The architecture has three components: a retriever that searches the external memory, a generative model that produces the response, and the external memory itself.

The alternative to RAG—providing the entire database as context or fine-tuning the model on it—is prohibitively expensive. A 20-terabyte database represents roughly 30 trillion tokens; computing attention over 30 trillion squared positions is computationally impossible. Fine-tuning on the full database costs millions of dollars and takes weeks. RAG provides an efficient middle path: retrieve only the relevant chunks and include them in the prompt.

3. Sparse Retrieval: Term-Based Methods

The oldest retrieval approach is term-based (lexical) search: match query terms against document terms and rank by relevance. TF-IDF (term frequency–inverse document frequency) scores how important a term is to a document relative to a corpus. BM25 (Best Matching 25, the 25th iteration of the algorithm from the 1990s) improves on TF-IDF by accounting for document length.

Elastic search, OpenSearch, and Redis Search all implement variants of term-based retrieval. For many practical applications—employee onboarding chatbots, leave policy lookups, FAQ systems—term-based search is entirely sufficient. If users will phrase their queries in predictable ways and the relevant documents contain the expected terms, there is no need for the complexity of embedding-based retrieval. A well-implemented elastic search system would have served most early RAG use cases better and more cheaply than the vector database architectures that were built to replace it.

4. Dense Retrieval: Embedding-Based Methods

When users phrase queries in diverse ways—where "hot dog" should not match queries about dogs—term-based retrieval fails because it cannot capture semantic meaning. Dense retrieval addresses this by embedding both documents and queries into the same vector space, then retrieving documents whose embeddings are closest to the query embedding.

The indexing pipeline splits documents into chunks (sentences, paragraphs, or pages), passes each chunk through an embedding model to produce a vector, and stores these vectors in a vector database. At query time, the query is embedded, and the database returns the K nearest vectors by cosine similarity.

Selecting the final set of chunks requires more than raw similarity. Maximum Marginal Relevance (MMR) balances relevance with diversity: if the top-5 most similar chunks all come from the same paragraph, they provide redundant context. MMR selects chunks that are individually relevant to the query but collectively diverse, covering different aspects of the topic.

Vector databases implement sophisticated indexing algorithms to make nearest-neighbour search fast at scale. HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) builds multi-layer proximity graphs for state-of-the-art recall-speed tradeoffs. FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) uses IVF (inverted file indexing with K-means clustering) combined with product quantization for billion-scale vector retrieval. LSH (locality-sensitive hashing) trades accuracy for speed through hash-bucket matching. Practical implementations include Chroma, Milvus, Qdrant, Pinecone, and Redis with vector search extensions.

5. From Naive RAG to Agentic RAG

Naive RAG—embed documents, embed queries, retrieve by similarity, generate—has significant limitations. The retriever may return irrelevant chunks. The chunking strategy may split critical information across chunk boundaries. The embedding model may not capture domain-specific semantics. The generative model may hallucinate even with correct context.

Hybrid RAG combines sparse and dense retrieval, using term-based matching for precision and embedding-based matching for recall, then merging and reranking results. This addresses cases where one method succeeds and the other fails.

Agentic RAG goes further: the retrieval process itself becomes an agent that can reformulate queries, retrieve from multiple sources, evaluate the quality of retrieved chunks, and iterate until sufficient context is gathered. Claude Code operates as an agentic RAG system—searching codebases, reading files, fetching web documentation, and building context before generating code. This agent-driven approach to context engineering represents the current state of the art and is the recommended pattern for new applications.

四、Summary

Post-training transforms a pre-trained transformer into a useful assistant through two mechanisms: supervised fine-tuning, which uses human-generated labels at high cost, and reinforcement learning from human feedback, which uses cheaper preference data processed through reward models or verifiable rewards. Context engineering—providing the right information alongside the right instructions—is how practitioners get useful results without retraining. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) retrieves relevant chunks from external memory using sparse (term-based) or dense (embedding-based) methods, while agentic patterns use autonomous search-and-retrieve loops. For most practical applications, the critical judgement is not which vector database to use but whether embedding-based retrieval is even necessary—term-based search handles the majority of real-world use cases. The progression from naive RAG through hybrid RAG to agentic RAG reflects the broader course theme: start simple, add complexity only where it demonstrably improves results, and always match the tool to the task.

Chapter 13: Agentic Design Patterns — From Augmented LLMs to Autonomous Agents

一、Introduction and Overview

Earlier chapters introduced agentic AI conceptually—what agents are, how they pursue goals, how Claude Code exemplifies full autonomy. This chapter systematises that knowledge into a pattern library: named, reusable solutions to recurring design problems in agentic systems. Just as data structures and algorithms provide the vocabulary for classical software engineering, agentic design patterns provide the vocabulary for AI-powered application development. The chapter establishes a three-level hierarchy of agency, examines each level's building blocks, and provides the decision framework for choosing the right level of autonomy for a given problem.

二、The Spectrum of Agency

1. Three Levels

Every agentic system sits somewhere on a spectrum defined by how much autonomy is delegated to the language model. The spectrum has three levels.

Level 1 is the augmented large language model. The LLM is enhanced with tools, memory, and structured output capabilities. It still responds to a single prompt, but it can reach beyond its training data by calling APIs, searching the web, executing code, or querying databases. Every modern chat interface—Claude.ai, ChatGPT, Gemini—operates at this level. There is some autonomy (the LLM decides which tools to call), but the scope is a single turn or a small number of tool-augmented turns.

Level 2 comprises workflow patterns. Multiple augmented LLM calls are orchestrated in a structured, deterministic sequence. The control flow—which calls happen, in what order, with what inputs—is defined by the engineer at build time, not decided by the LLM at runtime. The workflow is fixed; only the content processed at each step varies. This level covers an estimated 70-80% of practical AI applications.

Level 3 is the autonomous agent. The system pursues open-ended goals through self-directed loops of perception, planning, action, and evaluation. The number of steps, the tools called, and the strategies employed are all decided dynamically by the LLM. Claude Code operates at this level. This provides maximum flexibility but minimum predictability, at the highest token cost.

2. The Key Trade-Offs

As autonomy increases, predictability decreases. A Level 1 system produces deterministic, auditable outputs suitable for regulated industries. A Level 3 system may take surprising paths to reach its goal—powerful, but difficult to audit. Cost also increases with autonomy: more LLM calls, more tokens consumed per task, more reasoning overhead. Flexibility moves in the opposite direction: Level 1 handles only what was anticipated at design time, while Level 3 adapts to unforeseen situations.

The cardinal rule is: start at the lowest level that solves the problem. Do not build a Level 3 multi-agent system when a Level 1 augmented LLM with a well-crafted system prompt would suffice. Many proposed applications—waste segregation assistants, beginner investment advisors, FAQ chatbots—are Level 1 problems that require nothing more than a good prompt and a few tool calls. Recognising this is a critical exercise of taste.

三、Level 1: The Augmented LLM

1. Tool Calling Mechanics

In an augmented LLM, the user provides a prompt, and the LLM decides whether to call a tool. If it does, the generation pauses: the LLM emits a structured tool-call token (specifying which tool and what arguments), the harness (SDK) executes the tool, the result is injected back into the context, and the LLM resumes generation with the new information. This may repeat—multiple tools may be called in sequence before the final response is produced.

The LLM never executes tools itself. It only produces text that says "call this tool with these arguments." The harness—the SDK or agentic framework—handles actual execution. This separation between reasoning (LLM) and execution (harness) is fundamental to all agentic architectures.

2. Structuring the LLM Call

Seven components structure the context that flows into each LLM call. The system prompt defines the agent's identity, behaviour constraints, and available tools—it remains constant across calls. Optional few-shot examples demonstrate desired output style. Retrieved context (from RAG or memory) provides external knowledge. Conversation history maintains state across turns. The user prompt contains the actual task. After tool execution, tool results are injected. Optionally, a chain-of-thought prefix primes the reasoning style.

This structure is the atomic unit of all agentic systems. Every pattern at every level is composed of one or more of these structured LLM calls.

四、Level 2: Workflow Patterns

1. Prompt Chaining

In prompt chaining, the output of one LLM call feeds the next in a sequential pipeline. Each step refines or transforms the previous output. A typical chain might be: retrieve data from a database (first LLM call decides which SQL query to run), then analyse the retrieved data (second LLM call processes the results and produces insights). Each step may include a programmatic gate—a guardrail that checks whether the output meets required standards before passing it downstream.

Chaining is appropriate when each step refines or transforms the output of the previous step, and the sequence of steps is known in advance. Examples include translate-then-summarise-then-format pipelines, or retrieve-then-analyse workflows.

2. Routing

A routing pattern uses an LLM call (or a simpler classifier) to determine which specialist workflow should handle a given input. The router examines the user's query, classifies the intent, and dispatches to the appropriate branch. Only one branch executes per query.

This is the standard pattern for customer-facing applications. A credit card company's support system routes "I lost my card" to the fraud specialist, "Where is my refund?" to the refunds specialist, and "Cancel my order" to the cancellation specialist. Each specialist has its own system prompt, tools, and workflow optimised for its domain.

3. Parallelisation

When multiple independent sub-tasks must be completed and latency matters, parallelisation runs several LLM calls simultaneously. An aggregator collects and synthesises the results. Unlike routing (where one branch runs), all branches run in parallel.

Parallelisation is appropriate when the sub-tasks are genuinely independent—when the output of one does not depend on the output of another. If dependencies exist, chaining is the correct pattern.

4. Orchestrator-Sub-Agent

The orchestrator-sub-agent pattern adds intelligence to task decomposition. Instead of a fixed routing table, an LLM reasons about the task and dynamically decides which sub-agents to invoke and in what order. A synthesiser collects outputs from sub-agents and produces the final response.

This pattern costs more than simple routing (the orchestrator's reasoning consumes tokens) and is less predictable (the decomposition may vary between runs). It is appropriate when the task decomposition cannot be predetermined—when the nature of the sub-tasks depends on the specific input.

5. Evaluator-Optimiser

In this pattern, one LLM generates output and another evaluates it, providing feedback that drives iterative improvement. The generator produces a draft; the evaluator scores it against criteria; if the score is insufficient, the generator revises. This loop continues until the evaluator is satisfied or a maximum number of iterations is reached.

This is the pattern behind many quality-sensitive applications: code review agents, content refinement systems, and iterative design tools.

五、Level 3: Autonomous Agents

Autonomous agents operate in self-directed loops without predetermined workflows. The agent perceives its environment (reads files, observes outputs), plans its next action (reasons about what to do), acts (calls tools, writes code, sends messages), and evaluates the result (checks whether the goal is closer). This cycle repeats until the goal is achieved or the agent determines it cannot proceed.

Single-agent patterns give one agent full autonomy over a task. Multi-agent patterns distribute work across specialised agents that communicate with each other—an orchestrator agent delegating to researcher, coder, and reviewer agents, for example.

The power of Level 3 is flexibility: the agent can handle situations that were not anticipated at design time. The cost is unpredictability and token consumption. The discipline of specification-driven development—as covered in earlier chapters—is the mechanism that tames Level 3 autonomy, bringing predictability up and cost down without sacrificing flexibility.

六、Anti-Patterns and Decision Framework

The most common anti-pattern is premature escalation: jumping to Level 3 multi-agent systems when Level 1 or Level 2 would suffice. This wastes tokens, increases latency, reduces predictability, and adds engineering complexity—all without improving outcomes.

The decision framework is straightforward. Can the problem be solved with a single well-prompted LLM call with tool access? Use Level 1. Can the workflow be predetermined, even if individual steps require LLM reasoning? Use Level 2. Is the task genuinely open-ended, requiring dynamic planning and adaptation? Only then use Level 3.

These patterns are composable: a Level 2 workflow might use Level 1 augmented LLM calls at each step, and a Level 3 agent might invoke Level 2 workflows as sub-tasks. The patterns are building blocks—Lego pieces that snap together in whatever configuration the problem requires.

七、Summary

Agentic design patterns provide a systematic vocabulary for building AI-powered applications. Three levels of agency—augmented LLM, workflow, and autonomous agent—represent increasing autonomy, decreasing predictability, and increasing cost. Five workflow patterns—chaining, routing, parallelisation, orchestrator-sub-agent, and evaluator-optimiser—cover the vast majority of practical applications. The cardinal rule is to start at the lowest level that solves the problem and escalate only when simpler patterns demonstrably fail. These patterns compose freely: complex systems are built by snapping together simple, well-understood building blocks, each chosen for the specific sub-problem it addresses.

Chapter 14: Interoperability Standards and Agent Implementation

一、Introduction and Overview

The previous chapter provided the design patterns for building agentic systems. This chapter addresses the engineering infrastructure that makes those patterns practical at scale: the Model Context Protocol (MCP) for agent-tool interoperability, the Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol for inter-agent communication, and the concrete implementation of an agent loop using the Anthropic SDK. Through the barista agent example, it demonstrates how the abstract patterns translate into working code—and how real-world debugging exposes the gap between specification and implementation.

二、The Interoperability Problem

As agents proliferate, each needing access to multiple tools, the integration burden grows multiplicatively. If N agents must connect to M tools, and each connection requires custom integration code, the result is N × M integrations. Ten agents accessing 100 tools require 1,000 custom integrations—each with its own data format, error handling, and authentication logic.

A standard protocol reduces this to N + M: each agent implements the client side of the protocol, each tool implements the server side, and any client can connect to any server. The same principle that drove USB-C to replace dozens of proprietary connectors drives MCP to replace custom agent-tool integrations.

三、Model Context Protocol (MCP)

1. Architecture: Hosts, Clients, and Servers

MCP separates the ecosystem into three roles. The host is the application that embeds the language model—the agentic harness, the SDK, the user-facing application. The host manages the user interaction and the agent lifecycle. The client is a protocol implementation layer that lives inside the host. It speaks MCP, manages connections to one or more servers, and mediates between the LLM and external capabilities. The server is an external process that exposes tools, resources, or prompts via the MCP standard. It could be a database, a web search service, a file system, or any specialised capability.

The separation of concerns is the key insight. Tool developers build MCP servers that advertise their capabilities. Application developers embed MCP clients in their hosts. The LLM sees tool descriptions provided by the client, decides when to invoke tools, and the client-server protocol handles the rest. When a tool provider adds a new capability to their server, every connected client discovers it automatically—no code changes needed on the client side.

2. Three Primitives

MCP servers expose three types of capabilities. Tools are functions the LLM can call—search, compute, query a database, place an order. Resources are data the LLM can read—files, database rows, API responses. Prompts are reusable prompt templates that the server provides, encoding the prompt patterns that work best with its tools. The tool developer, who understands their system best, can specify exactly how the LLM should phrase its requests.

3. The Invocation Flow

When a user sends a prompt, the host assembles the context: system prompt, tool descriptions (obtained from MCP servers via the client), user input, and any conversation history. This context goes to the LLM. The LLM reasons about the task and may generate a tool-call token: "invoke tool X with arguments Y." The host detects this, passes the request to the MCP client, which forwards it to the appropriate MCP server. The server executes the tool and returns the result. The client passes the result back to the host, which injects it into the LLM's context. The LLM resumes generation, now informed by the tool's output.

This flow may repeat—the LLM might call multiple tools before producing its final response. The key property is that the LLM never executes tools directly. It only emits structured requests; the harness handles execution.

4. Transport Layers

For development and learning, MCP uses standard input/output (STDIO) transport—client and server communicate through pipes on the same machine. For production deployment, HTTP streaming serves as the transport, allowing clients and servers to reside on different machines communicating over the internet. The protocol itself (JSON-RPC 2.0) remains the same regardless of transport.

5. MCP in Practice: Before and After

Without MCP, an agent loop requires manual tool definitions (name, description, input schema written by hand), a dispatch function that routes tool calls to implementations via if-else logic, and manual serialisation of results. Each tool added requires changes to the agent's code.

With MCP, tools are defined as decorated Python functions on the server side. The @mcp.tool decorator automatically generates the schema from function signatures and docstrings, registers the function for client discovery, and handles serialisation. The agent loop simply asks the server what tools are available and lets the LLM decide which to call. Adding a new tool means writing one decorated function on the server—no changes to the client or agent loop.

四、Agent-to-Agent Protocol (A2A)

1. The Inter-Agent Problem

MCP solves agent-tool communication but not agent-agent communication. When Agent A (running on Anthropic's SDK with Claude) needs to delegate a sub-task to Agent B (running on Google's ADK with Gemini), no tool-calling protocol suffices. The agents need to exchange tasks, messages, and artifacts in a framework-agnostic manner.

The A2A protocol, introduced by Google and contributed to the Linux Foundation, standardises this communication. It defines three layers: a canonical data model (tasks, messages, agent cards, artifacts expressed as protocol buffer messages), abstract operations (send message, get task, list tasks, cancel tasks), and protocol bindings (JSON-RPC, gRPC, HTTP REST endpoints).

2. MCP and A2A Together

The two protocols are complementary. MCP handles the vertical axis: agents connecting downward to tools. A2A handles the horizontal axis: agents connecting sideways to other agents. A complete agentic system might use MCP for tool access and A2A for inter-agent delegation, regardless of which framework or model powers each agent.

As of mid-2026, MCP is widely adopted and production-ready. A2A is still under active development with limited real-world deployment. The trajectory is clear—build with any SDK, equip with MCP, communicate via A2A—but practitioners should currently invest more heavily in MCP competency.

五、The Agent Loop in Practice

1. The Core Loop

Every agent, regardless of framework complexity, runs the same fundamental loop. Initialise a message list with the user's input. Enter a while-true loop: send messages to the LLM with the system prompt and tool descriptions; if the response is "end turn," return the final answer; if the response is "tool use," execute the requested tool, append the tool call and result to the message history, and repeat.

This loop is the atomic building block of all agentic systems. Lang chain, LangGraph, CrewAI, and every other framework ultimately implement variations of this loop. Understanding it directly—before any framework abstraction—is essential for debugging and for recognising when a framework is adding value versus adding complexity.

2. The Barista Agent: A Worked Example

A barista agent demonstrates the loop in practice. Three tools are defined: get_menu (returns available drinks filtered by category), check_inventory (verifies a specific drink is in stock), and place_order (creates an order and returns an order ID). The system prompt establishes identity ("You are a friendly barista assistant") and constraints ("Always check inventory before placing an order").

When a user types "I want something cold," the LLM reasons that it needs the cold drinks menu, generates a tool call to get_menu with category "cold," the harness executes the tool and returns the drink list, and the LLM formulates a friendly response presenting the options.

3. Debugging Agentic Systems

The barista example reveals a class of bugs unique to agentic systems. When a user orders an "ice latte" and the inventory check returns "unavailable" despite the item being in stock, the root cause is a case mismatch: the menu stores "Iced Latte" but the LLM passed "ice latte" to the inventory check function, which performed an exact string match.

Three resolution approaches illustrate the spectrum of solutions. The simplest is case-insensitive matching in the tool implementation—fast and dependency-free, but fails on typos and near-matches. The second uses fuzzy matching (libraries like RapidFuzz) that handle misspellings and approximate matches at the cost of an additional dependency. The third and most modern approach adds a single instruction to the system prompt: "Always call get_menu first and use the drink name exactly as it appears in the menu." This offloads ambiguity resolution to the LLM itself, requiring no code changes to the tools.

This debugging episode encapsulates a broader principle: in agentic systems, bugs often live in the interface between the LLM's natural language reasoning and the tool's deterministic logic. The LLM operates in a fuzzy, approximate world of language; the tool operates in an exact, case-sensitive world of code. The specification—whether in the system prompt or in the tool's matching logic—must bridge this gap.

六、Summary

MCP and A2A provide the interoperability standards that allow agentic systems to scale. MCP separates tool providers (servers) from tool consumers (clients), reducing N × M custom integrations to N + M standard connections. A2A extends this principle to agent-agent communication across frameworks and models. The agent loop—the fundamental while-true cycle of LLM call, tool execution, and context update—is the atomic building block underlying every agentic framework. Implementing it directly, as in the barista example, reveals both the simplicity of the core pattern and the subtlety of the debugging challenges that arise at the boundary between natural language reasoning and deterministic tool execution. Understanding these foundations enables practitioners to choose the right level of abstraction—from raw SDK loops to full framework orchestration—for each application they build.

Chapter 15: Evaluating LLM Applications — The Analyse-Measure-Improve Loop

一、Introduction and Overview

Building an agentic application is only half the work. The other half—arguably the harder half—is knowing whether it actually works. This chapter addresses the evaluation of LLM-powered applications, a discipline that differs fundamentally from traditional software testing. It introduces the Three Gulfs mental model that explains why LLM pipelines fail, develops the Analyse-Measure-Improve loop as a systematic framework for discovering and fixing failures, and establishes the practical techniques—open coding, failure taxonomies, evaluator design, and LLM-as-judge—that constitute the emerging professional practice of agentic evaluation.

二、Why LLM Evaluation Is Hard

1. Four Properties That Break Traditional Testing

LLM pipelines violate every assumption of traditional software testing. First, they are non-deterministic: the same input can produce different outputs across runs, even with temperature set to zero, because floating-point arithmetic in parallel GPU operations introduces irreducible variation. Different hardware architectures (Ampere, Blackwell, Ada Lovelace), different CUDA versions, and even different PyTorch versions can produce different outputs from identical inputs. The assert_equal statement, the backbone of traditional testing, is dead on arrival.

Second, quality is subjective. Correctness in an LLM pipeline is not a binary property but a multidimensional judgment involving tone, relevance, completeness, safety, and factual accuracy. Two expert evaluators may disagree on whether a response is "good." The same response may be appropriate for one user and inappropriate for another.

Third, failures are silent. The system does not throw exceptions or produce stack traces. It replies fluently, confidently, and incorrectly. There is no automated signal that something has gone wrong—the developer must actively discover failures by reading outputs, which is expensive and does not scale.

Fourth, there are many moving parts. A single user interaction may involve multiple LLM calls, multiple tool invocations, routing decisions, and state updates. A bug in a tool argument three steps upstream may manifest as a wrong final answer, with no indication of where the chain broke.

2. Traditional Testing versus LLM Evaluation

In traditional software, output is deterministic, correctness means matching specification exactly, failure produces exceptions and wrong values, the test oracle is assert_equal, and problems are traceable to specific lines of code. In LLM pipelines, output varies between runs, correctness is a judgment call involving tone, relevance, and safety, failure produces fluent confident wrong answers, the test oracle requires human labels or LLM-as-judge, and problems could originate anywhere in the pipeline—or upstream in the API itself.

三、The Three Gulfs Mental Model

Three gaps—or gulfs—explain why LLM pipelines go wrong. The model, developed by Shreya Shankar and colleagues, identifies the interactions between the developer, the LLM pipeline, and the data as the sources of failure.

The gulf of comprehension lies between the developer and the data. The developer cannot manually read every input and output the system handles. At any meaningful scale, most of what the pipeline does is invisible. Failures hide in the traces that were never opened. The developer reads a handful of examples, builds biased intuition, and fixes issues that may not be the most urgent. The bridge across this gulf is systematic error analysis on a sampled, representative set of traces.

The gulf of specification lies between the developer and the LLM pipeline. The prompt is not the intent. When a developer writes "be concise," the model executes those words, not the unstated assumptions about what "concise" means, for whom, and in what context. Ambiguity makes behaviour inconsistent. The bridge is precise prompts with explicit definitions of what constitutes good output.

The gulf of generalization lies between the LLM pipeline and the data. The pipeline works on the ten development examples but fails on user number 1,047 who sends a typo, an ambiguous request, or multiple orders at once. Better models shrink this gulf but never close it completely. The bridge is diverse test datasets and continuous monitoring in production.

四、The Analyse-Measure-Improve Loop

The AMI loop is the systematic framework for bridging all three gulfs. Each iteration produces concrete deliverables that feed the next.

1. Analyse: Building the Failure Taxonomy

The analysis phase bridges the gulf of comprehension through two coding stages borrowed from qualitative social science research.

The first stage is open coding. The developer generates diverse synthetic user queries by sampling across dimensions of the user population—different drinks, sizes, milks, scenarios (well-formed orders, ambiguous requests, out-of-stock items, off-menu items, multiple items, misspellings). These queries are run through the system, and traces are collected. A trace is the complete record of one request: every LLM call, every tool call, every routing decision, every argument and result.

The developer reads each trace—not just the final response, but every span—and writes short, free-form notes describing what they observe. "Billed $4 but menu says $3.50." "Never asked which size, guessed medium silently." "Discount applied with no membership check." These are open codes: the developer's own words, with no predetermined categories. The goal is 2-4 observations per trace across at least 100 traces, with multiple passes.

The second stage is axial coding. The open codes are grouped, merged, and given precise names. "Silent mispricing" (wrong price billed without error). "Silent default" (meaningful attribute guessed, not asked). "Unjustified discount" (discount applied with no eligibility check). Each failure mode gets a one-line definition and a count of how many traces exhibit it. The target is 8-10 named failure modes.

The taxonomy distinguishes systemic failures (appearing in most traces, usually caused by a single prompt or code issue, fixable once to eliminate a whole class of problems) from sporadic failures (input-dependent, appearing in some traces, requiring targeted tests and continuous monitoring). The priority for measurement and improvement is frequency multiplied by impact.

Common pitfalls in analysis include jumping straight to fixes (analysis is for seeing clearly, not for patching), reading only final responses (failures live in tool arguments and routing decisions), using generic borrowed terms like "hallucination" (use taxonomy specific to your application), and stopping after one pass (later traces reveal failures missed in earlier ones).

When two developers label the same traces, disagreements reveal either ambiguous definitions (sharpen the taxonomy) or genuinely borderline cases (discuss, decide, and record the decision as precedent). Cohen's kappa provides a quantitative measure of inter-rater agreement.

2. Measure: From Taxonomy to Evaluators

For each failure mode in the taxonomy, one binary evaluator is designed. The evaluator answers a single pass/fail question: does this failure mode occur in this trace? Binary by design—severity scales come later.

Three types of evaluators exist, and the cheapest oracle always wins. Deterministic code checks are used when the failure can be detected programmatically: discount greater than zero with no loyalty flag is a simple comparison. Code-plus-lookup combines code with reference data: checking whether the billed price matches the menu price requires looking up the menu. LLM-as-judge is used only when the failure requires natural language judgment: whether a reply correctly distinguishes "out of stock" from "not on our menu" cannot be checked with code alone.

The cardinal rule is: if a code check can detect the failure, never spend an LLM call on it. LLM-as-judge adds cost, latency, and complexity. Use it only when no simpler evaluator suffices.

3. Building an LLM-as-Judge

When an LLM judge is needed, four components are essential. A clear criterion, scoped to exactly one failure mode: "Does the reply correctly communicate why the order could not be processed?" Precise pass/fail definitions copied verbatim from the taxonomy. Few-shot examples drawn from human-labelled traces, showing what pass and fail look like. A structured output format (JSON with a reasoning field) that makes verdicts machine-readable and auditable.

Designing a judge is equivalent to training a classifier by prompt engineering. The development-testing paradigm applies: few-shot examples come from a training pool, the prompt and criteria are refined against a development set (whose examples never appear in the prompt itself), and the final judge is evaluated on a held-out test set to produce unbiased true positive and true negative rates.

The temptation to use LLM-as-judge for every failure mode must be resisted. Using a model to evaluate its own outputs is like grading your own homework—the incentive structure is wrong, and the results are unreliable. Judges must be validated against human labels before they can be trusted.

4. Improve: Fix, Verify, Monitor

With failure modes named, counted, and measured, improvement follows the priority ordering established in analysis. Systemic failures are fixed first—often a single line in a system prompt or a missing field in a tool schema. Each fix is verified by re-running the evaluators on the affected traces to confirm the failure mode no longer triggers.

Critically, every fix must be checked for regressions. Fixing one part of a prompt may break another behaviour. The full suite of evaluators runs after every change, catching regressions that would otherwise go unnoticed. This is why evaluators are built for every failure mode, even ones that were fixed during development: model API updates, prompt changes, or upstream degradation can reintroduce previously fixed failures.

After deployment, new failure modes will appear. The AMI loop repeats: analyse new traces, update the taxonomy, build new evaluators, improve, and deploy again. This is continuous monitoring—the LLM equivalent of CI/CD, where the "T" stands for testing that never stops.

五、The Human Element

The AMI loop is not yet fully automatable. Using an agent to evaluate its own pipeline is circular—a more powerful evaluator model could simply be used as the application model. Humans remain essential for open coding (reading traces with fresh eyes), for defining failure taxonomies (naming requires domain expertise), for validating judges (ground truth requires human judgment), and for making priority decisions (frequency × impact requires business context).

This is precisely why the skill of LLM evaluation is professionally valuable. It combines domain expertise, product thinking, systematic methodology, and the willingness to do the careful, unglamorous work of reading traces and naming failures. It is the quality engineering discipline for the age of generative AI.

六、Summary

LLM applications fail silently, subjectively, and probabilistically—breaking every assumption of traditional software testing. The Three Gulfs model identifies comprehension (not seeing what the system does), specification (not saying what you mean), and generalization (not covering what users will do) as the root causes. The Analyse-Measure-Improve loop provides the systematic response: analyse traces through open and axial coding to build a failure taxonomy; measure each failure mode with the cheapest evaluator that works (code check, code-plus-lookup, or LLM-as-judge); improve by fixing failures in priority order and verifying with regression tests. The taxonomy is the deliverable—every metric, every evaluator, and every improvement decision flows from the named, counted, prioritised failure modes that systematic error analysis reveals.
